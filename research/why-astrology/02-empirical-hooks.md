# 02 — Empirical Hooks: The Sky Is Real, Physical, and Entangled (Not Predictive)

## How to read this dossier

**Confidence key** (applied per claim, not to the dossier as a whole):

- **✔︎ solid** — peer-reviewed, replicated or a settled literature, mainstream scientific consensus.
- **○ real-but-partial** — a genuine, verifiable finding, but narrower / more mechanistic / less
  poetic than the popular version; the popular gloss overclaims even though something real sits
  under it.
- **◆ contested** — an active scientific dispute exists. Both sides are stated. Do not resolve it
  in course copy; present the dispute itself as the honest position.

Where a claim is contested, **both sides are given, in the same entry** — no cherry-picking the
side that's more useful for the course's story. This mirrors how the tarot course handled mixed
historical records: state the disagreement, don't launder it.

## 1. Trees and plants communicate in channels we cannot perceive

### 1.1 Mycorrhizal networks ("the wood wide web") — ◆ contested

**The finding that started it:** Suzanne Simard's doctoral research used radioactive-carbon
tracers to show sugars moving between Douglas fir and paper birch seedlings connected by shared
ectomycorrhizal fungi — when one tree was shaded, it received extra carbon via the fungal network.
Published as Simard, S.W., Perry, D.A., Jones, M.D., Myrold, D.D., Durall, D.M. & Molina, R.
(1997), "Net transfer of carbon between ectomycorrhizal tree species in the field," *Nature*
388, 579–582, doi:10.1038/41557. *Nature* ran it under the cover headline "The Wood-Wide Web"
(a phrase coined by commentator David Read, not Simard herself) — the popular metaphor traces to
that headline, not to the data. **Confidence: ✔︎** for the original tracer result itself (net
carbon transfer measured in the field between two species sharing a fungal partner).

**The 2023 critique:** Karst, J., Jones, M.D. & Hoeksema, J.D. (2023), "Positive citation bias and
overinterpreted results lead to misinformation on common mycorrhizal networks in forests,"
*Nature Ecology & Evolution* 7, 501–511, doi:10.1038/s41559-023-01986-1 (an Author Correction
followed: doi:10.1038/s41559-023-02035-7, correcting citation-count figures, not the conclusion).
The authors audited **>1,500 papers** citing common-mycorrhizal-network (CMN) claims and found:
the claim that CMNs are **widespread in forests** is insufficiently supported (field results vary
too widely / have alternative explanations); the claim that CMNs **increase seedling
performance** is not well supported; and the claim that mature "mother trees" **preferentially
send resources and defense signals to their own offspring** through CMNs has **no peer-reviewed
published evidence** at all. They also found a **citation bias that doubled over 25 years** —
papers increasingly cite CMN effects as established fact even as the underlying evidence stayed
thin.

**Present both sides in course copy:** (a) A real, measured, peer-reviewed transfer of carbon
between two tree species sharing a fungal network exists and is not disputed [@simard1997]. (b)
The leap from that finding to "forests are a communicating, cooperating, mother-tree-nurturing
superorganism" — the popular "wood wide web" story — outruns the evidence, according to a 2023
audit of the literature by researchers in the same field [@karst2023]. **Confidence on the
overall popular claim: ◆ contested / overclaimed.** Use this as the cautionary example of how a
real finding becomes myth through media amplification — a useful parallel to how the course
should NOT talk about astrology.

### 1.2 Airborne ultrasonic plant sounds — ✔︎ solid, mechanism unclear

Khait, I., Lewin-Epstein, O., Sharon, R., Saban, K., Goldstein, R., Anikster, Y., Zeron, Y.,
Agassy, C., Nizan, S., Sharabi, G., Perelman, R., Boonman, A., Sade, N., Yovel, Y. & Hadany, L.
(2023), "Sounds emitted by plants under stress are airborne and informative," *Cell* 186(7),
1328–1336.e10, doi:10.1016/j.cell.2023.03.009.

Tomato and tobacco plants under dehydration stress or with a cut stem emit **airborne clicking
sounds in the ~20–100 kHz range** — above human hearing (ultrasonic) — at roughly **30–50 pops
per hour** (vs. far fewer in unstressed plants), detectable from **3–5 meters** away with normal
microphones. Machine-learning models trained on the recordings could classify plant condition
(dehydration level, physical injury) from the sound alone. The proposed physical mechanism is
**cavitation** — the formation and violent collapse of air bubbles in the plant's water-transport
vasculature (xylem) under stress — though the authors are explicit the exact mechanism is not yet
settled. The paper notes the sounds are plausibly within the hearing range of insects, other
mammals, and possibly other plants, but **does not claim plants hear or respond to them** — that
is a separate, unresolved question the paper flags rather than answers. **Confidence: ✔︎** for the
acoustic measurement and classification result; **○** for any claim about plant-to-plant or
plant-to-insect *reception* of the sound (real hypothesis, not yet demonstrated).

### 1.3 Volatile organic compound (VOC) signaling between plants — ✔︎ solid, well-reviewed

Plants release blends of volatile organic compounds under herbivory or other stress, and
neighboring plants can "eavesdrop" on these airborne cues to prime their own defenses before
being attacked themselves. Solid review: Ninkovic, V., Markovic, D. & Rensing, M. (2021),
"Volatile-mediated plant–plant interactions: volatile organic compounds as modulators of receiver
plant defence, growth, and reproduction," *Journal of Experimental Botany* 73(2), 511–528,
doi:10.1093/jxb/erab487. This is one of the more mechanistically mature "plants sense the world in
channels we don't" findings — VOC priming is broadly accepted in plant science, with active
research on the specific receptors and signaling pathways involved. **Confidence: ✔︎** for the
existence and ecological function of VOC-mediated inter-plant signaling as a documented
phenomenon; **○** for how general/universal the effect is across species (still an active research
area, per the review's own stated open questions).

## 2. The sky is a real biological clock and a real physical driver

### 2.1 Coral mass spawning cued by the moon — ✔︎ solid (mechanism actively being refined)

Scleractinian coral species across reefs worldwide synchronize mass spawning to within days each
year, timed relative to the full moon — a textbook case of a genuine celestial cue driving mass
biological behavior. Corals sense lunar light via **cryptochrome photoreceptor proteins**
sensitive to blue light; gene and protein expression cycles with the lunar cycle. Key sources:
Kaniewska, P. et al. (2015), "Signaling cascades and the importance of moonlight in coral
broadcast mass spawning," *eLife* 4:e09991, doi:10.7554/eLife.09991 (PMC4721961); and more
recently Hongo, C. et al. / Komoto, H., Lin, C.-H., Nozawa, Y. & Satake, A. (2023), "An External
Coincidence Model for the Lunar Cycle Reveals Circadian Phase-Dependent Moonlight Effects on Coral
Spawning," *Journal of Biological Rhythms*, doi:10.1177/07487304221135916 (PMID 36461677); also
Fukushiro et al./ Shiu et al., "Moonrise timing is key for synchronized spawning in coral
*Dipsastraea speciosa*," *PNAS* (2021), doi:10.1073/pnas.2101985118 (PMC8403928). **Nuance worth
keeping honest**: different coral species use *different* mechanisms — moonlight itself is the
main driver for some (e.g. *Acropora*), while for others (e.g. *Dipsastraea speciosa*, a
merulinid) moonlight actually **suppresses** spawning and it's the **darkness window** between
sunset and moonrise that triggers it. The precise trigger within the lunar cycle for some species
remains an open research question. **Confidence: ✔︎** that lunar/light cues genuinely drive mass
coral spawning timing; **○** on the exact universal mechanism (it is species-specific and still
being mapped).

### 2.2 Heliotropism: sunflowers track the sun via a real circadian mechanism — ✔︎ solid

Atamian, H.S., Creux, N.M., Brown, E.A., Garner, A.G., Blackman, B.K. & Harmer, S.L. (2016),
"Circadian regulation of sunflower heliotropism, floral orientation, and pollinator visits,"
*Science* 353(6299), 587–590, doi:10.1126/science.aaf9793. Young sunflowers track the sun
east-to-west during the day and reorient east overnight in anticipation of dawn — driven by an
internal circadian clock producing antiphasic growth (differential elongation) on the east vs.
west sides of the stem, not simply a passive light-following reflex. Mature plants stop tracking
and fix east-facing. East-facing flowers run warmer and attract measurably more pollinators — so
the clock-driven behavior has a real fitness payoff. **Confidence: ✔︎.** This is one of the
cleanest "an organism's body plan is wired to a celestial rhythm, and the wiring is a real,
identified cellular clock, not folklore" cases in biology.

### 2.3 Honest counterweight — the human "lunar effect" is mostly null — ✔︎ solid (as a null result)

The claim that the full moon affects human behavior (births, ER visits, crime, sleep, psychiatric
admissions, "lunacy") is the single most-tested folk-astrological claim, and it does not hold up.
Rotton, J. & Kelly, I.W. (1985), "Much ado about the full moon: A meta-analysis of lunar-lunacy
research," *Psychological Bulletin* 97(2), 286–306. Across 37 studies, lunar phase accounted for
**no more than ~1% of variance** in the behaviors studied (hospital admissions, psychiatric
crises, crisis-line calls, homicides, other crime); the few statistically significant results
were inconsistent and explainable by confounds (uncontrolled weekly cycles, publication bias
toward positive findings). The belief persists disproportionately among people who also score
higher on belief in ESP, reincarnation, and astrology, and lower on tests of logical reasoning —
i.e., the belief tracks general paranormal credulity more than it tracks any real signal. Sleep is
the one place a *plausible, mundane* mechanism exists — brighter moonlit nights and historically
more nocturnal outdoor light — but even here the evidence is mixed and modest, not the dramatic
effect folklore claims. **State this plainly and without hedging in course copy**: the sky's most
famous claimed effect on humans specifically is the one that mostly isn't there. This is the
dossier's core "we are not claiming prediction" anchor.

## 3. Gravity and tides: a real coupling, of the wrong magnitude for astrology

Gravity and tidal forces from the sun, moon, and planets are **real, measurable, physical
phenomena** — this is uncontroversial physics, not something to soften. What the classic
skeptical calculation shows is the **magnitude problem**: these forces are real but astronomically
smaller than astrology's causal story requires. The widely cited calculation (Culver, R.B. &
Ianna, P.A. (1988), *Astrology: True or False? A Scientific Evaluation*, Prometheus Books; popularized in Andrew
Fraknoi's essay "An Astronomer Looks at Astrology," Astronomical Society of the Pacific): **the
obstetrician (or any adult) standing at the bedside during a birth exerts roughly six times the
gravitational pull, and about two thousand billion (2×10¹²) times the tidal force, of the planet
Mars on the newborn** — because gravitational and tidal force fall off steeply with distance, and
proximity beats planetary mass by many orders of magnitude. **Confidence: ✔︎** for the physics
(gravity/tidal force scale with mass and inverse distance in exactly this way — this is basic,
uncontested Newtonian mechanics) and **✔︎** that the specific comparison has been made and
re-published by working astronomers as a standard rebuttal. **The honest framing for the course**:
don't say "gravity/tides connect us to the planets" as if that licenses astrology's causal claims
— say the forces are **real but negligible at planetary distance/mass**, so the "shared clock" and
"real forces exist" claims are true, while "a planet's gravity shapes your personality at birth" is
not physically supportable. Keep these two claims sharply separate; conflating them is exactly
the overclaim this dossier exists to prevent.

## 4. Friedman's "as-if": a licensed bridge from mechanism to model

Milton Friedman, "The Methodology of Positive Economics," in *Essays in Positive Economics*
(University of Chicago Press, 1953). Friedman's central argument: a hypothesis's value lies in
its *predictive power*, not in the literal realism of its assumptions. His illustrative examples
(quoted/paraphrased from the essay, confidence ✔︎ on content, wording assembled from
multiple secondary sources reproducing the original — **recommend the course team verify final
wording against the primary text before quoting Friedman directly on-page**):

- **The billiard player**: "It seems not at all unreasonable that excellent predictions would be
  yielded by the hypothesis that the billiard player made his shots **as if** he knew the
  complicated mathematical formulas that would give the optimum directions of travel... Our
  confidence in this hypothesis is not based on the belief that billiard players... can or do go
  through the process described; it derives rather from the belief that, unless in some way or
  other they were capable of reaching essentially the same result, they would not in fact be
  expert billiard players."
- **The leaves on a tree**: Friedman proposes that leaves around a tree are positioned "**as if**
  each leaf deliberately sought to maximize the amount of sunlight it receives, given the position
  of its neighbors," and "as if it knew the physical laws determining the amount of sunlight" and
  "could move rapidly or instantaneously from any one position to any other desired and unoccupied
  position" — a transparently false literal mechanism (leaves don't deliberate or teleport) that
  nonetheless yields correct predictions (leaves are observably denser on the sunward side of a
  tree, and redistribute if that side is shaded). The real mechanism — phototropin blue-light
  receptors and differential cell growth — is not what the "as if" model invokes, yet the "as if"
  model predicts the pattern correctly.

**Why this is load-bearing for the course**: Friedman's point is precisely the shape needed for
"relate to the planets as if they are agents/beings." Science's actual mechanism for a leaf
turning toward light is a cellular photoreceptor, not intention — but treating the leaf "as if"
it wants sunlight is a useful, predictively adequate model of the tree's shape, and using it
requires no belief that leaves have minds. The course can say: **we relate to Venus or Saturn
"as if" they are characters with temperaments, the way an economist treats a firm "as if" it
maximizes profit or a billiard player "as if" he does calculus — the usefulness of the "as if"
does not require, and should not be confused with, a claim that the literal mechanism is
intentional agency.** **Confidence: ✔︎** that this is Friedman's argument and that these are his
two central illustrations; the essay is widely taught and these two examples (billiard player,
leaves) are the standard citations for it.

## 5. Festivals, synchrony, and collective effervescence as real coordination power

**Durkheim's collective effervescence.** Émile Durkheim, *The Elementary Forms of the Religious
Life* (1912; Eng. trans. 1915). Durkheim's term for the heightened emotional/energetic state
produced when a group gathers in physical proximity for shared ritual — a "kind of electricity"
that (in his account) is the actual generative mechanism behind the feeling of the sacred and the
reinforcement of social bonds. Widely used in anthropology and sociology since; a recent
meta-analytic review situates it as a real, measurable social-psychological phenomenon rather than
just a period metaphor: see the meta-analytic review of collective-gathering research in
*Frontiers in Psychology* (2022), "Emotional processes, collective behavior, and social
movements," PMC9473704. **Confidence: ✔︎** that this is an established, still-actively-used
sociological concept with a real empirical literature behind it (not merely Durkheim's own
armchair theorizing).

**Synchrony causally increases cooperation — an experimental result, not just theory.**
Wiltermuth, S.S. & Heath, C. (2009), "Synchrony and Cooperation," *Psychological Science* 20(1),
1–5, doi:10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02253.x. Across three controlled experiments, people who moved
in physical synchrony with others (walking in step, singing/moving together) **subsequently
cooperated more** in economic games requiring personal sacrifice for the group — even when the
synchronous activity itself produced no extra positive emotion. This is a genuine causal,
experimental result (not merely correlational or anecdotal), and it is the mechanistic ground
under "collective ritual really does build cooperation," independent of whatever the ritual's
professed content is about. **Confidence: ✔︎.**

**Calendars/festivals as social-coordination technology.** The anthropological point (well
supported across the discipline, though harder to pin to one single citation — treat as a
consensus view rather than one paper's claim): a shared calendar of festivals is a coordination
technology — it lets a dispersed group synchronize labor, ritual, exchange, and rest without
central control, simply by everyone tracking the same externally visible signal. **Confidence:
○ → ✔︎** as a general anthropological consensus (strong, but this dossier does not point to one
single canonical empirical paper the way the other claims do — a fuller citation pass on
historical/anthropological calendar-coordination literature, e.g. work in the vein of E.P.
Thompson's "time-discipline," would strengthen this specific sub-claim; flagged as an open item
below).

**Why this matters for the course**: the visible sky (new moon, full moon, solstices,
planetary risings) is the most universally *available*, zero-infrastructure shared signal a
dispersed community could ever coordinate around — before clocks, before writing, before the
state, everyone everywhere could see the same moon on the same night. The claim the course can
honestly make: **festivals and synchronized ritual measurably build cooperation and social bonds
[Durkheim; Wiltermuth & Heath 2009], and the sky is the original, free, universally shared clock
around which humans have coordinated that ritual life** — a claim about coordination technology
and social psychology, not about the planets exerting mystical influence.

## Quotable facts for course writers

1. A newborn's attending obstetrician exerts about **6× the gravitational pull and ~2 trillion
   times the tidal force of Mars** on the baby — the forces are real, the astrology-scale
   magnitude is not. *(Culver & Ianna 1988, popularized by Fraknoi, "An Astronomer Looks at
   Astrology.")*
2. Stressed tomato and tobacco plants **click at 20–100 kHz** — ultrasonic, inaudible to us — up
   to **30–50 pops per hour**, and a machine-learning model can tell *why* the plant is stressed
   just from the sound. *(Khait et al. 2023, *Cell* 186(7):1328–1336.)*
3. Young sunflowers reorient to face **east again overnight**, before dawn, driven by an internal
   circadian clock — not simply "following" the sun during the day. *(Atamian et al. 2016,
   *Science* 353(6299):587–590.)*
4. A meta-analysis of **37 studies** found the phase of the moon explains **no more than 1%** of
   variance in human "lunacy" — the most tested folk-celestial claim about human behavior is
   mostly a null result. *(Rotton & Kelly 1985, *Psychological Bulletin* 97(2):286–306.)*
5. A 2023 audit of **>1,500 papers** found the specific claim that "mother trees" preferentially
   nurture their own seedlings through fungal networks has **zero peer-reviewed evidence** — even
   though the underlying phenomenon (carbon moving between two tree species sharing a fungal
   symbiont) was genuinely measured in 1997. *(Simard et al. 1997, *Nature* 388:579–582; Karst,
   Jones & Hoeksema 2023, *Nature Ecology & Evolution* 7:501–511.)*
6. Milton Friedman argued a tree's leaves are arranged "**as if** each leaf deliberately sought to
   maximize the amount of sunlight it receives" — a model useful for correct prediction, built on
   a mechanism (deliberate leaves) everyone agrees is literally false. *(Friedman, "The
   Methodology of Positive Economics," 1953.)*
7. In three controlled experiments, people who merely **moved in physical synchrony** with
   strangers went on to cooperate more in a costly economic game afterward — synchrony causes
   cooperation, measured in the lab, not just claimed by ritual theorists. *(Wiltermuth & Heath
   2009, *Psychological Science* 20(1):1–5.)*
8. More than **130 coral species** synchronize mass spawning to within days of each other, once a
   year, cued by moonlight sensed through blue-light-sensitive cryptochrome proteins — a real
   molecular mechanism linking a lunar cue to mass reproductive behavior. *(Kaniewska et al. 2015,
   *eLife* 4:e09991.)*

## Synthesis (what the course can honestly claim)

What's real: the sky is a genuine, physically measured driver of life — coral spawning timed to
moonlight, sunflower growth timed to an internal solar clock, gravity and tides that are actual
forces, not metaphors. Living things also communicate in channels we cannot sense unaided — plant
ultrasound, plant volatile signaling — proof our unaided perception is not the limit of what's
happening around us. Shared festivals, timed to a universally visible sky, measurably build
cooperation. And Friedman's "as-if" gives real philosophical cover for relating to planets as
characters without claiming they are literally agents. What must NOT be overclaimed: the one
human behavioral effect most directly analogous to astrology's claims — lunar effects on human
behavior — is the one that mostly isn't there, and gravitational forces from planets are real but
many orders of magnitude too small to plausibly shape a life. Entanglement and real forces, yes.
Prediction, no.
