## 1. Jyotiṣa — `grammars/jyotisha-brihat-jataka/grammar.json`

**Primary source:** Varāhamihira, *Bṛhat Jātaka* (Sanskrit, c. 6th century CE), trans. N. Chidambaram Iyer (Foster Press, Madras, 1885). **Public domain** — pre-1930, translator confirmed. Held on archive.org at two identifiers: [archive.org/details/wg1079](https://archive.org/details/wg1079) and [archive.org/details/brihatjatakavar00iyergoog](https://archive.org/details/brihatjatakavar00iyergoog) (Google/Harvard scan). A widely-cited secondary note (jyotishbooks.wordpress.com) gives the translation's date as 1905, from a Thompson & Co./Theosophist Department reprint — the 1885 Foster Press edition is the earlier, primary printing this grammar cites; the two dates likely refer to first edition vs. a later reprint of the same translation (○ — not independently reconciled this session).

**Accessible mirror used for content this session:** [chestofbooks.com/new-age/astrology/Brihat-Jataka/](https://chestofbooks.com/new-age/astrology/Brihat-Jataka/index.html), which reproduces the Chidambaram Iyer translation chapter-by-chapter under Chapter I ("Definitions and Elementary Zodiacal Principles," covering the 12 rāśis) and Chapter II ("Definitions and Elementary Planetary Principles," covering the 9 grahas). All quoted phrases in the grammar (planet colors, body/temperament descriptions, Kālapuruṣa role assignments, sign colors, quadruped/biped/aquatic/centiped classifications, movable/fixed/common groupings, directional trigons) were surfaced via WebSearch queries targeting this mirror. Confidence: ○ across the board — corroborated by repeated, consistent WebSearch snippets, but not independently re-fetched from the mirror or the archive.org scan this session.

**Honest gaps:**
- **Rāhu and Ketu** (the two chāyā grahas / lunar nodes) — this session found solid material on their names (Tamas, Agu, Asura for Rāhu; Śikhin for Ketu) and the severed-asura eclipse myth from general Jyotiṣa reference sources, but did **not** locate a specific Chapter-II passage from the Chidambaram Iyer translation itself describing them the way it describes the seven classical planets. Both grammar items say so explicitly (confidence: ◆) rather than inventing a translation quote that wasn't found.
- The transmission-history question (how much of horoscopic Jyotiṣa derives from Hellenistic contact) is **not** re-litigated here — see the existing `research/views/jyotisha.md` dossier (confidence: low, Pingree vs. Mak debate) and the `view-jyotisha` grammar item it backs. This new grammar is a primary-source voice library, parallel to `grammars/tetrabiblos-ashmand` and `grammars/alan-leo`, not a re-verification of that historiographical debate.
- Cross-references to other grammars in this repo (`grammars/tetrabiblos-ashmand`, `grammars/alan-leo`, `grammars/western-astrology-canonical`, `grammars/planetary-myths`) are used throughout the "What this lens reads" sections to make the sidereal/tropical and cross-tradition comparisons concrete — these point at content already in the repo, not new claims.

## 2. Mesopotamian Omen-Craft — `grammars/mesopotamian-omens/grammar.json`

**Primary sources, both public domain:**
- R. Campbell Thompson, *The Reports of the Magicians and Astrologers of Nineveh and Babylon in the British Museum*, Vol. II (London, 1900) — a translated edition of the actual Neo-Assyrian scholars' letters/reports to the king, the closest thing to primary omen-report text this session could reach in a confirmed-PD form. [archive.org/details/reportsofmagicia07thomuoft](https://archive.org/details/reportsofmagicia07thomuoft).
- Stephen Langdon & J. K. Fotheringham, *The Venus Tablets of Ammizaduga* (Oxford University Press, 1928) — the earliest full published translation of Enūma Anu Enlil Tablet 63 (the Venus Tablet of Ammiṣaduqa). [archive.org/details/TheVenusTabletsOfAmmizaduga1928](https://archive.org/details/TheVenusTabletsOfAmmizaduga1928). **Note:** the later, more scholarly Reiner & Pingree edition (*Babylonian Planetary Omens*, 1975 onward) is explicitly **not** public domain and was not used as a source for any quoted material here.
- Morris Jastrow, Jr., *The Religion of Babylonia and Assyria* (1898) — used for deity background (Ištar/Dilbat, Sin/Nanna, Šamaš/Utu). Public domain, also on [Project Gutenberg #20758](https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/20758).

**Quoted material obtained via WebSearch** targeting `therealsamizdat.com`'s extensive category-page excerpting of Thompson's 1900 translation — this secondary site reproduces many individual omen-report lines verbatim with citation to Thompson Vol. II, London, 1900. Confirmed omen quotes for **Sin (Moon)**, **Šamaš (Sun)**, **Nergal (Mars)**, and **Marduk (Jupiter, including his phase-names Sagmigar/Nibiru/Umun-pa-uddu)** — all confidence: medium (corroborated across multiple independent search snippets pointing at the same named primary source).

**Honest gaps (stated directly in the grammar, not papered over):**
- **Ištar/Venus** — the Venus Tablet's existence and general contents (recording first/last visibility dates across a 21-year cycle) are well corroborated, but no specific verbatim protasis-apodosis line from the 1928 Langdon–Fotheringham translation was located this session. The item is written as an honest paraphrase with no invented quote (confidence: low on the quote specifically, medium on the surrounding facts).
- **Ninurta/Saturn** — this is the weakest item in the set. Saturn's Akkadian planetary name (Kayamānu) and a scholarly note that some omen-series glosses equate Saturn's path with "the path of the Sun" were found, but no specific omen line naming Ninurta/Saturn was located. The god-planet identification itself is noted in the secondary literature as inconsistently attested across tablets and periods — the item says this plainly (confidence: ◆/low) rather than fabricating a clean mapping.
- **Nabû/Mercury** — similarly thin. Mercury's fast motion and proximity to the Sun likely explain a genuinely smaller surviving omen corpus; the item states this as the probable reason for the gap and draws on Nabû's well-attested myth/cult profile (already present in this repo's `grammars/planetary-myths`) rather than inventing an omen quote (confidence: low).

## 3. Renaissance / Early Modern — `grammars/renaissance-lilly/grammar.json`

**Primary source:** William Lilly, *Christian Astrology, Modestly Treated of in Three Books* (London, 1647). Public domain (Creative Commons Public Domain Mark per the Internet Archive listing). Available at [archive.org/details/christian-astrology-1647](https://archive.org/details/christian-astrology-1647) and several other archive.org identifiers, plus a Wikisource edition.

**Supplementary source:** *An Introduction to Astrology* (1852), William Lilly's original text as heavily abridged and modernized-spelling-edited by "Zadkiel" (Richard James Morrison) — also public domain, hosted in full at [sacred-texts.com/astro/aia/](https://sacred-texts.com/astro/aia/index.htm). This is **not** identical wording to the 1647 original in all places (Zadkiel modernized spelling and trimmed material), so every quote in the grammar is labeled with which of the two editions WebSearch's synthesis attributed it to, and the Sun item — sourced only via the Zadkiel abridgment this session — is flagged at lower confidence than the six items corroborated against apparent verbatim 1647 spelling (via classicalastrologer.com's chapter-by-chapter reproduction, which preserves archaic capitalization and spelling like "Vertuous," "compleat," "stedfast").

**Confidence by item:** Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Venus, and the Moon — medium (quotes corroborated across multiple independent secondary reproductions of what appears to be verbatim 1647 text). Mercury and the Sun — low (Mercury's quote is short and single-sourced; the Sun's quote comes only from the 1852 Zadkiel abridgment, not confirmed against 1647 phrasing this session).

**Honest note on scope (as of Jul 7 2026):** Lilly's *Christian Astrology* is a three-book, several-hundred-page treatise covering houses, aspects, horary technique, and nativities far beyond planetary significations. This grammar deliberately covered only the seven planets at first (matching that day's task brief and this repo's existing pattern in `grammars/tetrabiblos-ashmand` and `grammars/alan-leo`, which are similarly planet/sign-scoped voice libraries, not full-book digitizations). **Update Jul 8 2026: the twelve zodiac signs were added in a follow-up pass — see "3b" below.** A houses or aspects companion grammar from the same source remains a possible future addition.

## 4. The excluded node — `sun-sign-naylor`

**Not built, and not buildable under this repo's public-domain rule.** R. H. Naylor's *Sunday Express* "Your Stars" column (1930–1952) is the primary source for the `view-sun-sign-press` chapter, but:
- It is a mid-20th-century British newspaper column. UK crown/newspaper copyright for such material generally runs to 70 years past the author's death (Naylor died 1952) or, for some categories, a fixed term from publication — either way, it remains under copyright as of 2026, not public domain.
- No PD translation or lapsed-copyright edition exists (unlike the two-century-plus-old Lilly and Chidambaram Iyer texts, or the pre-1930 Mesopotamian-omen translations).
- The existing `view-sun-sign-press` grammar item in `grammars/historiographies-of-astrology/grammar.json` already handles this honestly: it quotes only the two short, widely-reproduced Naylor lines ("eventful"... "a British aircraft will be in danger") via secondary retrospectives, explicitly flagged `○ reported, not ✔︎ primary-verified`, and its Research note says WebFetch returned 403 on the primary sources in an earlier session too.

**Conclusion:** no new `sun-sign-naylor` grammar was created. The gap is structural (copyright, not effort), and the existing course chapter already states the gap honestly rather than fabricating a primary-source excerpt for a still-copyrighted text. If a truly PD example of tabloid sun-sign astrology surfaces later (e.g. a pre-1930 American newspaper astrology column, which would be a different, earlier node than Naylor's specific 1930 event), that would be the way to fill this without infringing.
