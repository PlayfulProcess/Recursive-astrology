Research dossier for the strengthening pass on the `historiographies-of-astrology` grammar and
the "History of Astrology" course it drives. Compiled via WebSearch; WebFetch returned HTTP 403
on every target attempted this session (Wikipedia, Project Gutenberg, dokumen.pub, academia.edu,
theglorifiedtomato.com, mentalfloss.com — a proxy/anti-bot issue on this session's egress, not a
content problem). All findings below are therefore drawn from WebSearch result
snippets/summaries, cross-checked across multiple independent sources, rather than full-page
fetches. This matches the confidence caveat already on file in `01-wallis-devotion.md` for a
prior session — the same access limitation recurred here.

Confidence key: **✔︎** well-sourced (direct quote or clearly-attributed paraphrase from a
citeable, cross-corroborated source) · **○** plausible-but-thin (secondary characterization,
single source, or reported quote not independently checked against the primary text) · **◆**
contested-or-could-not-verify (excluded from load-bearing claims).

## Sources used this session, by chapter

### Jyotiṣa (`view-jyotisha`)
- ✔︎ "Eye of the Veda" traditional description of Jyotiṣa among the Vedāṅgas — corroborated
  across multiple sources including India's official Vedic Heritage Portal
  [vedicheritage.gov.in/vedangas/jyotisha](https://vedicheritage.gov.in/vedangas/jyotisha/).
  Presented in the grammar explicitly as a received traditional metaphor, not a single
  author's verbatim sentence.
- Existing dossier sources (Pingree 1997, Pingree 1978, Mak 2013, Campion 2008) unchanged;
  the transmission-question confidence stays `low`, as before.

### Islamic Golden Age (`view-islamic-golden-age`)
- ○ Paraphrase of Abū Maʿshar's *Great Introduction* opening defense (astrology likened to
  medicine — both natural arts reasoning from visible cause to fallible effect), following the
  standard scholarly description of the argument. Could not obtain a short, citable verbatim
  line from the Yamamoto & Burnett edition (Brill, 2019; not public domain, and WebFetch to
  dokumen.pub / academia.edu was blocked). Marked explicitly as paraphrase in the grammar text,
  not verbatim — consistent with how this grammar already treats the Ptolemy/Tetrabiblos
  paraphrase.

### The Great Decline & the Demarcation (`view-decline-demarcation`)
- ✔︎ Jonathan Swift's 1708 Bickerstaff hoax against astrologer John Partridge — the "infallibly
  dye upon the 29th of March" prediction — is a real, verbatim, well-documented quotation from
  Swift's *Predictions for the Year 1708*. Corroborated across academic coverage (Project MUSE:
  "There Is No Such Man as Isaack Bickerstaff") and popular historical accounts
  ([hoaxes.org](https://hoaxes.org/archive/permalink/the_predictions_of_isaac_bickerstaff)).
  Public-domain primary text exists at Project Gutenberg #1090 ("The Bickerstaff-Partridge
  Papers") but could not be refetched directly this session (403). High confidence via
  cross-source agreement even without a direct refetch.

### The Contemporary Resurgence (`view-contemporary-resurgence`)
- ○ Chris Brennan's preface line from *Hellenistic Astrology* ("only an introduction to a vast
  field") — found via a book-review site
  ([sevenstarsastrology.com](https://sevenstarsastrology.com/book-review-hellenistic-astrology-by-chris-brennan-the-study-of-fate-and-fortune/))
  that quotes the preface directly. Not checked against the print/ebook edition this session.
  Marked in the grammar as reported, not primary-verified.

### The Sun-Sign Press (`view-sun-sign-press`, new chapter)
- ✔︎ R. H. Naylor, *Sunday Express*, "What the Stars Foretell for the New Princess," 24 August
  1930 — well corroborated across many independent sources (Wikipedia via WebSearch synthesis,
  [Mental Floss](https://www.mentalfloss.com/article/634011/princess-margaret-modern-horoscope-inspiration),
  Literary Hub, Grokipedia, Prabook). Facts agreeing across sources: editor John Gordon;
  Naylor standing in for Cheiro; Naylor's dates 1889–1952; the "Your Stars" 12-sign system by
  1937; the R101 airship prediction and its October 1930 crash; Nicholas Campion (this
  bibliography's own historiographical spine) later inheriting the *Daily Mail* column from
  John Naylor, R. H. Naylor's son.
- ○ The exact quoted wording of both 1930 predictions ("eventful," "events of tremendous
  importance... near her seventh year," "a British aircraft will be in danger") is consistent
  across secondary sources but was not checked against the original clipping — flagged as such
  in both the grammar item and its dossier.
- ◆ Excluded: any numeric/market claim about astrology's current popularity (no survey data
  sourced) and any claim that Naylor's forecasts constituted genuine foresight rather than
  vague-then-matched retrospection — the grammar explicitly reads this the same way the
  Empirical Test Tradition view would.

## What was excluded

  already applies via `view-empirical-test-tradition`.
- No verbatim Abū Maʿshar quotation — a real paraphrase, clearly marked, was used instead of
  inventing or half-remembering a translation.
- No market-size or survey claim added anywhere (matches the existing discipline in
  `contemporary-resurgence.md` §5).
