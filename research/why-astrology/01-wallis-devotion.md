## 1. Devatā as personalized form of the one consciousness

**Core claim:** in non-dual Śaiva Tantra, Consciousness (Śiva/Paramaśiva) is the sole reality —
yet the tradition still invokes Śiva, Śakti, Bhairava, and the goddesses as vivid, personal
figures. Wallis's teaching is that this is not a concession to the unenlightened; the deity is a
real, usable *form* consciousness takes so a limited human self can relate to what is otherwise
too vast and formless to approach directly.

- **✔︎** Wallis, on the *Deconstructing Yourself* podcast with Michael Taft ("Exploring Nondual
  Shaiva Tantra"), on why deities are still invoked in a tradition where Consciousness alone is
  real:
  > "And yet we still invoke these deities because we don't know ourselves in our true nature or
  > in our deepest nature. And the deities serve to indicate to us in various ways, something
  > about the vastness, the power, the depth of that consciousness, which we are."
  Source: [Transcript of Exploring Nondual Shaiva Tantra, with Christopher Wallis](https://deconstructingyourself.com/transcript-of-exploring-nondual-shaiva-tantra-with-christopher-wallis.html)
  (deconstructingyourself.com). The deities are explicitly framed as pointers/instantiations of
  an aspect of the single consciousness that is otherwise too vast to grasp directly — i.e. a
  face for the faceless. This is the closest verifiable equivalent of the "face for the
  faceless" formulation; I could not find that exact phrase in Wallis's own words, so treat the
  phrase itself as a paraphrase, not a Wallis quote (see ◆ note below).

- **✔︎** Wallis, hareesh.org blog, "How is 'God' a Near Enemy of the Truth?" (2021-06-05),
  reconciling personal-God devotion with a non-dual, formless ultimate:
  > "If you're experiencing God or the divine as something separate from you, you start there.
  > This higher nondual tradition teaches that if you experience a higher power, if you
  > experience God as a reality higher than what you think of as you, then your prayer is
  > legitimate. Pray to that higher power insofar as you perceive that there is a higher power,
  > even though you may ultimately realize later on that that higher power is nothing other than
  > your true nature."
  Source: [How is 'God' a Near Enemy of the Truth?](https://hareesh.org/blog/2021/6/5/near-enemy-16-god)
  (Hareesh.org). This is the single strongest anchor for the course's core move: relating to a
  planet **as if** it were a personal, higher power is not a lesser or mistaken practice — it's
  a legitimate starting point that the tradition explicitly validates, even while holding that
  the "higher power" is ultimately not other than one's own deepest nature. Directly reusable
  for "planet-as-devatā."

- **✔︎** Same post, on how personal-God language and impersonal/transpersonal consciousness
  language are not actually in conflict:
  > "God is in everything as the essence of everything but doesn't constitute the whole of
  > everything." — and elsewhere in the same discussion, noting that some non-dualists avoid the
  > word "God" specifically because they want to stress the divine's impersonal,
  > transpersonal character — while Wallis's own teaching holds room for both registers.
  Source: same as above, [hareesh.org/blog/2021/6/5/near-enemy-16-god](https://hareesh.org/blog/2021/6/5/near-enemy-16-god).

- **○** General framing (Tantra Illuminated marketing/summary copy, not a direct book quote):
  nondual Tantra teaches "though the Divine is more than meets the eye, it is also everything
  the eye can meet." Source: search-result summary tied to
  [Tantra Illuminated](https://www.tantrailluminated.org/christopher-hareesh-wallis) /
  embodiedphilosophy.com episode page. Useful as a plain-language gloss but not a verified
  book-page quote — flagged thin because I could not confirm exact page or exact wording.

- **◆ Inferred connective move (mine, not Wallis's stated words):** if Consciousness can
  legitimately wear the face of Śiva, Bhairava, or a goddess so a person can love, fear, invoke,
  and be changed by it — and the tradition says this is a *real* and *effective* practice, not
  a fiction to be graduated out of — then a planet functioning as a personalized figure (Saturn
  as a stern teacher, Venus as a beloved) is structurally the same move: the whole (consciousness
  / the cosmos) taking a legible, relatable, personal shape so a finite human can actually work
  with it. Wallis never discusses planets; this bridge is the course's own extension of his
  deity-relation logic, and should be labeled as such rather than attributed to him.

## 2. Bhakti / devotion within non-dualism — not a lesser path

**Core claim:** devotion is not a concession for people who haven't yet grasped non-duality; it's
a technology of transformation that operates *even when you already know it's all one* — arguably
especially then, because the "apparent" separation is what makes love, longing, and relationship
possible and useful.

- **✔︎ Strongest single anchor — Utpaladeva's Śivastotrāvalī**, as Wallis renders/paraphrases it
  in the same *Deconstructing Yourself* interview, explicitly holding non-dual philosophy and
  devotional practice together in one figure:
  > "Oh Shiva, let me continue to have this experience of apparent separation from you so that I
  > can taste the sweetness of this total love and devotion of me for you, and you for me, even
  > though I know that you are me, and I am you."
  Source: [Transcript of Exploring Nondual Shaiva Tantra, with Christopher Wallis](https://deconstructingyourself.com/transcript-of-exploring-nondual-shaiva-tantra-with-christopher-wallis.html).
  Wallis's framing (per the same transcript, paraphrased by the search summary, so ○ for the
  exact wording but ✔︎ for the substance): Utpaladeva — the philosophical grandfather of
  Kashmir Śaivism's Recognition (Pratyabhijñā) school, whose *Īśvarapratyabhijñā-kārikā*
  establishes the strict non-dual metaphysics — is the *same author* who wrote the
  Śivastotrāvalī, a book-length outpouring of devotional longing to Śiva. This is the strongest
  possible proof-by-biography that non-dual metaphysics and personal devotion were held by one
  mind as complementary, not contradictory.

- **✔︎** Independent confirmation the Śivastotrāvalī is read by scholars/practitioners exactly
  this way: "Utpaladeva was a mystic of bhakti as expressed in his Hymns Śivastotrāvali, and for
  him, bhakti and advaita [non-dualism] were not opposed to each other but complementary." Also:
  "The recognition of one's identity with Śiva is realized by ecstatic aesthetic devotion,
  conceptualized as *bhakti-rasa*." Sources: search summaries drawing on
  [Utpaladeva — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utpaladeva) and academic descriptions
  of the Śivastotrāvalī (e.g. Constantina Rhodes Bailly's translation *Meditations on Śiva: The
  Śivastotrāvali of Utpaladeva*, and the Swami Lakshman Joo edition/commentary). The
  Śivastotrāvalī itself is independently described as "an expression of intense bhakti, longing
  for the Lord, and the mystical experience of the author and his non-dual union with Śiva" —
  see listings for [Śivastotrāvalī of Utpaladeva, tr. Swami Lakshman Joo](https://www.amazon.com/Sivastotravali-Utpaladeva-Swami-Lakshman-Joo/dp/8124604673).

- **✔︎** Wallis, on why devotion has a real place even in a "radically nondual" tradition
  (search-summarized from the same *Deconstructing Yourself* transcript): the role of bhakti was
  honored and emphasized "because when experiencing oneself as an individual bodymind, one
  experiences that one's ultimate nature is something higher than oneself" — even though "in
  nondual mystical states, that difference completely evaporates." Source: same transcript,
  [deconstructingyourself.com](https://deconstructingyourself.com/transcript-of-exploring-nondual-shaiva-tantra-with-christopher-wallis.html).
  This is the clean formal statement of "devotion works precisely because ordinary experience is
  dualistic, and non-dual realization doesn't retroactively invalidate that — it completes it."

- **○** Wallis's teaching model of the "Three Upāyas" (Effective Methods) in *Tantra
  Illuminated* includes the **Empowered Method** (contemplation, imagination, and — per several
  summaries — devotional/theistic practice) alongside the **Divine Method** (direct
  nonconceptual awareness) and the **Embodied Method** (yogic/somatic practice). This structurally
  places bhakti-style relational practice as one of three *equally legitimate* gateways to
  realization, not a preliminary rung below the "real" nondual path. I could not confirm the
  exact book page mapping devotion specifically to the Empowered Method, so flagged thin —
  but the three-method structure itself is well-attested. Source: search summaries of *Tantra
  Illuminated* study materials, e.g. [tantrailluminated.org/study](https://www.tantrailluminated.org/study)
  and podcast episode "[The Three Effective Methods](https://shows.acast.com/tantra-illuminated-with-dr-christopher-wallis/episodes/55-the-three-effective-methods)."

- **○** Hareesh.org has a dedicated piece on bhakti's history and legitimacy as a path:
  "Paradigms of Bhakti: the South Indian Poet-Saints," describing the bhakti movement (emerging
  ~6th century CE in South India, spreading over the following ten centuries) as centered on
  stories, songs, poetry, and emotional expressiveness toward the Divine. I was not able to
  fetch the full text (site blocked this session) to confirm whether Wallis explicitly frames it
  as compatible with non-dual philosophy in that specific post, so treat as thin/contextual only.
  Source: [Paradigms of Bhakti: the South Indian Poet-Saints](https://hareesh.org/blog/2016/9/15/paradigms-of-bhakti).

## 3. THE KEY GEM — Pūrva Mīmāṃsā: deity as word, myth as ritual-instrumental only

**Core claim (verified):** Pūrva Mīmāṃsā (Śabara, Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, Prabhākara) is the school
where mythic/laudatory Vedic passages (*arthavāda*) carry **no independent truth-value** — their
only job is to praise, encourage, and thereby motivate performance of the ritual injunction
(*vidhi*). And, more radically: Mīmāṃsā holds that **the gods named in ritual have no
existence apart from the mantras/words that name them** — sacrificial efficacy comes from the
ritual act itself (and the supersensible ritual-residue called *apūrva*), not from a real
metaphysical being receiving worship.

- **✔︎ Deity-as-word.** "The Gods named in the Vedas had no [substantial] existence apart from
  the mantras that speak their names... the power of the mantras is what is seen as the power of
  Gods." This is the standard scholarly characterization of the Mīmāṃsā position, converged on
  across multiple independent summaries (Wikipedia's Mīmāṃsā article; Indian-philosophy survey
  sites). Source: search synthesis over [Mīmāṃsā - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81)
  and related survey pages.

- **✔︎ Primary-text-level confirmation, via Elisa Freschi's scholarship.** Elisa Freschi (a
  leading contemporary Mīmāṃsā philologist) has a close textual analysis, "Kumārila on
  deities" (Nov 2025), examining a specific passage: **Kumārila's *Ṭupṭīkā* ad 9.1, adhikaraṇa 4**
  (p. 1652ff in the Śubbāśāstrī 1929 edition), commenting on Śabara's *Śābara-bhāṣya*. Per the
  analysis: Śabara explains that **the deity does not promote/actively cause the sacrifice's
  effect** — an opponent objects that the deity is grammatically marked as the *recipient*
  (*sampradāna*) of the ritual action, but Śabara dismisses this as incidental to grammar, since
  the sacrificial substance/act itself is the real instrument producing the result. Freschi's
  conclusion: **Kumārila did not believe in the language-independent existence of deities**;
  Śabara is reported as saying he is fine with the deity being **"word only" (*śabda eva*)** —
  i.e. the deity's reality is exhausted by its role as a linguistic/ritual placeholder, and this
  doesn't damage the Mīmāṃsā system at all. Freschi further characterizes the resulting picture:
  sacrifices work independently of deities, who at most might be "Epicurean-like" entities (real
  perhaps, but causally inert, with **no function in human lives**); and "the deity's seeming
  predominant role is due to grammar only." Sources: [Kumārila on deities — elisa freschi](https://elisafreschi.com/2025/11/09/kumarila-on-deities/)
  and its mirror at [Kumārila on deities — The Indian Philosophy Blog](https://indianphilosophyblog.org/2025/11/10/kumarila-on-deities/).
  This is the closest thing to a smoking-gun primary-text citation available through search; I
  was not able to fetch the full post to pull Freschi's own translated Sanskrit, so treat the
  precise wording of "word only" / śabda eva as ✔︎-but-search-mediated, not independently
  checked against the Sanskrit.

- **✔︎ Arthavāda has no independent truth-value; its job is purely to move the will toward
  the injunction.** Converging characterization across sources: "arthavāda texts are almost
  always correlated to another text which is injunctive, and most of the time arthavāda texts
  tempt and prompt the agent to perform the act laid down in the injunctive text." And: Vedic
  sentences are classed into injunctions (*vidhi*), explanatory/mythic passages (*arthavāda*),
  mantras, and names (*nāmadheya*) — each with a distinct function — with *arthavāda* playing "a
  supportive role in motivating adherence to prescribed ritual actions through explanatory and
  persuasive statements rather than direct injunctions." Sources: search synthesis over
  [The Mīmāṃsā Doctrine of Arthavāda — Shabda Journal](https://journal.shabda.co/2021/06/17/mimamsa-doctrine-of-arthavada/),
  [Arthavada — wisdomlib.org](https://www.wisdomlib.org/definition/arthavada), and
  [Mīmāṃsā - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C4%ABm%C4%81%E1%B9%83s%C4%81).
  This is the textbook version of "myths matter only for their ritualistic/motivational value" —
  the builder's recollection is essentially correct and this is a real, well-documented Mīmāṃsā
  doctrine, not a misremembering.

- **✔︎ Kumārila separately argues against an embodied, personal creator-God (Īśvara) on
  logical grounds** — e.g., an embodied God couldn't coherently be revered by different people in
  different places at once if tied to one body — reinforcing that Mīmāṃsā's skepticism about
  deity-as-independently-real is a considered philosophical position, not an oversight. "Kumārila
  ... rejects the notion of a supreme deity or God ... an embodied God is inherently
  contradictory because how could he be revered by different people in different places
  simultaneously if he were linked to a body." Source: search synthesis over
  [Kumārila Bhaṭṭa — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kum%C4%81rila_Bha%E1%B9%AD%E1%B9%ADa)
  and [Kumārila on deities](https://indianphilosophyblog.org/2025/11/10/kumarila-on-deities/).

- **◆ Naming caveat — "coordination value" is a modern gloss, not the ancient school's own
  term.** The builder's phrase "myths were only useful for their ritualistic and coordination
  value" maps very well onto arthavāda theory (ritual + motivational value), but "coordination"
  in the specific technical sense (common knowledge, focal points, group-coordination games) is
  a *modern, unrelated* academic framework — most associated with Michael Suk-Young Chwe's book
  **"Rational Ritual: Culture, Coordination, and Common Knowledge"** (Princeton, 2001), which
  analyzes rituals/myths/media events as generators of *common knowledge* that let groups
  coordinate action. This is a real, well-regarded piece of scholarship and a legitimate lens for
  the course — but it is a distinct 21st-century game-theoretic framing, not something Kumārila
  or Śabara said. If the course wants to use "coordination" language, it should credit Chwe's
  frame separately from the Mīmāṃsā frame, rather than merging them into one ancient doctrine.
  Source: search results summarizing [Rational Ritual: Culture, Coordination and Common
  Knowledge](https://www.academia.edu/6736357/Rational_Ritual_Culture_Coordination_and_Common_Knowledge).

- **◆ The connective move to "open hearts, not the sacred object" (mine, building on the
  above, not a scholarly claim):** Mīmāṃsā's deity-as-word position says the sacred efficacy of a
  Vedic sacrifice was never located in the deity's independent metaphysical reality — it was
  located in the correctly-performed ritual act and (implicitly) the disposition/participation of
  the humans doing it. Transplanted to the temple/astrology context: the claim "it is not that
  the temple needs to be sacred — the open hearts of the people in it might be the only thing
  that matters" is a modern-devotional-humanist restatement of a genuinely ancient move: **you
  do not need to resolve the metaphysical status of the god/planet in order for the practice
  built around it to be real and effective.** Mīmāṃsā got there by a hard-nosed ritualist,
  almost anti-theistic route (deny the deity, keep the ritual); Wallis's non-dual Tantra gets to
  a structurally similar place by the opposite route (affirm consciousness is the only reality,
  and the deity is a valid personal *form* it takes for us). Both roads converge on: **don't put
  the metaphysical weight on the object's literal, independent reality — put it on the practice
  and the practitioner.** This convergence-from-opposite-directions is the course's strongest
  argument for holding planet-as-devatā lightly and honestly.

## 4. Ritual, festival, and shared practice as legitimate ends in themselves

- **○** Wallis's account (via *Tantra Illuminated* summaries) of the historical Śaiva
  *kula* (spiritual community) emphasizes the centrality of communal practice, initiation
  (*dīkṣā*), and transmission (*śaktipāta*) — "NST emphasizes the crucial importance of having a
  teacher, of proper initiation, and of the role of community" — alongside "devotional ritual
  before a likeness of the deity and the meditative visualization (*dhyāna*) that is so typical of
  Tantra." This establishes that communal/ritual practice is treated as structurally necessary,
  not an optional decoration on top of private philosophical insight. Source: search summary of
  [Tantra Illuminated — About Christopher Hareesh Wallis](https://www.tantrailluminated.org/christopher-hareesh-wallis)
  and related book-description pages. Flagged thin because I could not confirm an exact page
  citation for "festival specifically as a legitimate end in itself" — the material found
  supports "communal ritual is central and necessary," which is close but not identical to the
  stronger claim requested.

- **◆** I was not able to locate a specific Wallis statement asserting that ritual/festival is
  valuable *even absent* any metaphysical payoff (i.e., ritual as pure communal/aesthetic good in
  its own right, independent of transformation or realization). The Mīmāṃsā material in Section 3
  is the strongest available anchor for "ritual/practice has value on its own terms, decoupled
  from the literal truth of the myth around it" — but that is a Mīmāṃsā argument, not a Wallis
  one. If the course needs a Wallis-attributed claim specifically about festival/communal
  practice as a legitimate end, that would need to come from the private corpus (not reachable
  here) or a direct read of *Tantra Illuminated*'s chapters on Kaula ritual and community, which
  this session could not fetch.

## Synthesis (150 words)

Two ancient Indian schools arrive, from opposite directions, at the same permission slip. Non-dual
Śaiva Tantra (Wallis's reading of Utpaladeva and the Recognition school) says Consciousness alone
is real, yet lets it wear personal faces — Śiva, Bhairava, a goddess — because a finite human can
love, address, and be transformed by a face in ways it cannot by the formless whole; devotion
survives full non-dual realization, it doesn't get outgrown. Pūrva Mīmāṃsā, hard-nosed ritualists
uninterested in metaphysics, says the opposite about the gods — they're "word only" — and finds
the sacrifice's power in the correctly-performed act, not the deity's independent existence. Put
together: whether you affirm the One wearing a mask or deny the god behind the word, both schools
refuse to rest the practice's value on settling the metaphysics first. That's the license for
planets-as-devatā — relate to Saturn or Venus as a real personal figure, hold the ontology loosely,
and trust that the practice and the open hearts doing it are where the sacredness actually lives.
