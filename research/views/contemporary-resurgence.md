# The Contemporary Resurgence

## 1. At a glance

Since the 1990s astrology has surged again on two levels at once. On top: the pop layer —
birth-chart and horoscope apps, "what's your big three" as small talk, a dense meme culture
of the signs, astrology as ambient vocabulary of self-description [@campion2012]. Underneath:
a serious technical revival — translation projects putting Hellenistic, medieval, and Arabic
sources into modern languages, and practitioners reviving older methods (whole-sign houses,
time-lord systems) that the psychological century had set aside [@brennan2017]. Because this
is contemporary history — still being written, involving living people — everything in this
dossier is provisional, lightly held, and kept at `confidence: low` on purpose.

## 2. Chronology & geography

- c. 1993 onward: the translation-revival efforts gather pace — Project Hindsight and
  successor projects making primary sources available to practitioners; Brennan's book is
  both a product and a history of that revival [@brennan2017].
- 2010s onward: the app-and-meme boom in the Anglophone internet; Campion's study of
  astrology's place in modern popular religion frames the demand side [@campion2012].
- Present: the two layers feed each other — revived ancient technique circulates through the
  same platforms as the memes. No endpoint; the dossier should be re-dated at every pass.

## 3. Key texts & figures

- **Chris Brennan, *Hellenistic Astrology* (2017)** — the revival's self-history and the
  accessible account of the recovered techniques [@brennan2017].
- **Nicholas Campion, *Astrology and Popular Religion in the Modern West* (2012)** — the
  scholarly frame for the popular layer [@campion2012].
- **Figures: none named.** The repo's rule — name a school, not a living person — bites
  hardest here. Movements and projects may be named (Project Hindsight, as a project); the
  people building them appear only when the record and the rule both allow.

## 4. What this view holds (in its own terms)

In its own terms the resurgence is a *recovery*: the conviction that the tradition's older
strata — fate-frank Hellenistic technique, medieval method — were richer than the diluted
sun-sign inheritance, and that the sources themselves, retranslated, are the authority
[@brennan2017]. The pop layer holds something simpler: that the chart is a shareable language
of identity and relation — less a doctrine than a social vocabulary [@campion2012]. Both are
real; they should not be collapsed into each other, and the grammar's single item holds them
side by side deliberately.

## 5. The record vs. the claims

- **"Astrology is bigger than ever"** — market and survey claims circulate widely but are
  hard to source solidly; this dossier makes no numerical claim at all
  (confidence: low as unstated specifics; add sourced survey data or say nothing).
- **The revival's relation to fate** — the recovered Hellenistic material is franker about
  fate than the psychological century was; how living practitioners metabolize that is
  contemporary religion, not settled history [@brennan2017; @campion2012]. Observe; don't
  adjudicate.
- **Why now** — theories abound (digital platforms, institutional religion's retreat,
  precarity). Campion offers frameworks [@campion2012]; none is established enough to state
  as the cause.

## 6. The autonomy note (the creed reads this era)

The resurgence contains both of the creed's poles at once: mirror-language self-description
that is almost pure creed, and revived fate-technique that tests it. This library's stance
stays what it is everywhere — record the practice honestly, endorse only the autonomy floor:
read the sky to know yourself, not to be told your fate. Stated as history-in-progress.
