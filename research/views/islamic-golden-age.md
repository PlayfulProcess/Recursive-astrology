# The Islamic Golden Age

## 1. At a glance

Under the Abbasids, astrology is preserved, systematized, and advanced as a court science and
a branch of natural philosophy. The Graeco-Arabic translation movement (c. 8th–10th centuries,
Baghdad) renders Greek, Persian, and Sanskrit astral learning into Arabic [@gutas1998];
astrologers such as Abū Maʿshar (Latin: Albumasar, d. 886 CE) join astrology to Aristotelian
physics and to history itself through the doctrine of great conjunctions [@abumashar2019;
@campion2009]. When this material flows into Latin Europe from the 12th century, it is the
channel through which systematic astrology returns to the West [@campion2009; @tester1987].

## 2. Chronology & geography

- The translation movement flourishes in Baghdad c. the 8th–10th centuries CE; Gutas is the
  standard account of its scale and social embedding [@gutas1998].
- Abū Maʿshar works in 9th-century Baghdad; his *Great Introduction* (*Kitāb al-madkhal
  al-kabīr*) becomes the medieval world's most comprehensive astrological treatise
  [@abumashar2019].
- al-Bīrūnī writes *The Book of Instruction in the Elements of the Art of Astrology* at
  Ghazna in 1029 CE — a primer that covers mathematics and astronomy before astrology,
  showing where the art sat in the curriculum of the sciences [@biruniwright1934].
- From the 12th century, Latin translators (Toledo and elsewhere) carry this corpus into
  Europe; Abū Maʿshar's Latin fortunes are the emblematic case [@campion2009; @tester1987].

## 4. What this view holds (in its own terms)

Astrology here is ʿilm aḥkām al-nujūm — the science of the judgments of the stars — a learned
discipline sitting beside astronomy, mathematics, and medicine in the court and the curriculum
[@gutas1998; @campion2009]. Abū Maʿshar's *Great Introduction* opens by *defending* astrology
philosophically on Aristotelian grounds — celestial motion as intermediary cause in the
sublunary world — precisely because the art had learned critics from the start
[@abumashar2019]. The grand ambition is historical: the theory of great conjunctions reads
the rise and fall of dynasties and religions in the slow clock of Jupiter–Saturn meetings
[@campion2009]. And al-Bīrūnī's primer shows the other face: the astrologer must first be a
competent astronomer and mathematician [@biruniwright1934].

## 5. The record vs. the claims

- **"The Arabs merely preserved Greek astrology"** — false as stated. The synthesis is
  genuinely new: Persian historical astrology, Indian material, and original doctrine (lots,
  interrogations, the conjunctions framework) are woven in [@gutas1998; @campion2009;
  @pingree1997].
- **Religious status** — astrology was practiced at the highest courts *and* attacked by
  theologians and philosophers within Islam; both are part of the record. (Kept general here;
  a load-bearing claim about any specific jurist's ruling needs its own source —
  confidence: medium.)
- **al-Bīrūnī's own stance** — often described as personally skeptical while writing the
  standard primer; state with care and cite the primer itself rather than lore about it
  [@biruniwright1934] (confidence: medium).

## 6. The autonomy note (the creed reads this era)

The era's own philosophers kept the question of celestial *causation* versus *signification*
alive, and its astrologers wrote defenses because critique was live [@abumashar2019]. The
creed reads the great-conjunctions doctrine as this view's grandest mirror — history examined
in the sky's slow calendar — and declines, as ever, the deterministic reading the court
sometimes wanted. Stated as history, not endorsement.
