# The Theosophical Revival

## 1. At a glance

Around 1900, astrology is reborn in Britain through the Theosophical movement, above all in
the work of Alan Leo (1860–1917): mass-market simplification, an organizational base (lodges,
magazines, correspondence courses), and — decisively — a reframing of the art away from
event-prediction toward character and spiritual evolution, with karma and reincarnation as
the metaphysics [@curry1992; @campion2009; @leo1913]. This moralizing, psychologizing turn is
the hinge on which the whole modern "inner" astrology swings; the twentieth century's
psychological astrology is unthinkable without it.

## 2. Chronology & geography

- 1890s–1910s: Leo's publishing and organizing decades — *Modern Astrology* magazine, the
  shilling manuals, the Theosophical lodge context [@curry1992; @campion2009].
- 1913: *Esoteric Astrology* states the mature system — the chart read for the soul's
  evolution [@leo1913].
- 1914 and 1917: Leo is prosecuted under fortune-telling law; the second case ends in
  conviction and a fine, and the historiography reads his late doctrinal turn — away from
  concrete prediction, toward "tendencies" and character — as shaped partly by that legal
  pressure as well as by Theosophical conviction [@curry1992] (case details at
  confidence: medium — take specifics from Curry, not from lore).
- Leo dies in 1917; his corpus is public domain, which makes this view unusually quotable for
  a free educational site [@leo1913].

## 4. What this view holds (in its own terms)

In Leo's own terms the horoscope is a map of the soul's condition in this incarnation: the
planets show karma ripening, and the point of reading a chart is *growth*, not fortune-telling
[@leo1913]. The slogan the movement compressed this into — **"character is destiny"** — is
widely attributed to Leo in the historiography as his watchword; the grammar deliberately
flags it as a movement slogan rather than a sourced verbatim sentence, and this dossier keeps
that flag until someone locates the exact printed instance [@curry1992; @campion2009]
(confidence: medium on verbatim wording; high on the reframing it names).

## 5. The record vs. the claims

- **The legal-pressure reading** — Curry's account connects Leo's 1914/1917 prosecutions to
  his retreat from event-prediction [@curry1992]. It is an *interpretation* (a good one);
  present it as the historiography's reading, not as Leo's own testimony.
- **"Leo invented modern astrology"** — half-true. The revival is also organizational and
  commercial (courses, magazines, cheap manuals), and it is Theosophy's metaphysics doing the
  reframing, not one man alone [@curry1992; @campion2009].
- **Continuity with the almanac trade** — the revival grows out of a Victorian popular
  astrology (Raphael's and Zadkiel's almanacs) that never died; Curry's *Confusion of
  Prophets* is the study [@curry1992]. The decline dossier hands off to this one here.

## 6. The autonomy note (the creed reads this era)

This is the era in which astrology itself starts saying something like the creed: character
over event, growth over fate, tendencies over decrees [@leo1913; @curry1992]. The creed
notes the irony honestly: the turn was partly forced by prosecutors, and partly a genuine
Theosophical conviction — motives the record cannot fully separate. Either way, the modern
"mirror" reading of the chart begins here. Stated as history, not endorsement.
