# Mesopotamian Omen-Craft

## 1. At a glance

The oldest documented layer of the astrological tradition: celestial divination practiced in
Mesopotamia across the second and first millennia BCE, in which events in the sky — eclipses,
planetary risings, halos, conjunctions — were read as signs written by the gods concerning the
king and the land [@rochberg2004; @kochwestenholz1995]. It is state-craft, not self-knowledge:
there is no birth chart for most of this history, and the reading's purpose is care of the realm.
The personal horoscope appears only at this world's very end.

## 2. Chronology & geography

- Omen material with celestial content is attested from the Old Babylonian period (early 2nd
  millennium BCE) onward; the tradition is Mesopotamian (Babylonia and Assyria), with later
  diffusion beyond [@rochberg2004; @hungerpingree1999].
- The canonical celestial-omen series *Enūma Anu Enlil* — conventionally counted at around 70
  tablets — took shape by c. the late second millennium BCE and was copied and consulted for
  centuries after [@hungerpingree1999; @kochwestenholz1995]. Its redaction history is
  reconstructed from fragmentary and dispersed tablets and remains debated in detail
  (confidence: medium — the broad picture is secure; tablet counts and dating of recensions are not).
- Systematic astronomical observation runs alongside: the Neo-Assyrian court correspondence
  (8th–7th c. BCE) shows scholars reporting omens to the kings, and the Babylonian astronomical
  diaries record observations over many centuries [@rochberg2004; @hungerpingree1999].
- The earliest surviving personal horoscopes from Babylonia date to 410 BCE — the two oldest
  natal texts, from Babylon and Nippur, in Rochberg's edition of the roughly thirty surviving
  cuneiform horoscopes [@rochberg1998]. This is the hinge moment when the sky begins to be
  read for an individual birth rather than for the state (verified against the edition's
  catalogue description 2026-07-05; confidence: high).

## 3. Key texts & figures

- ***Enūma Anu Enlil*** — "When (the gods) Anu (and) Enlil…", the great omen series; entries in
  the conditional form "if X is seen in the sky, then Y (in the land)" [@kochwestenholz1995].
  No integral PD English translation of the whole series exists; editions are specialist and
  piecemeal (Phase 3 `texts/` entry should say exactly this rather than link a substitute).
- **The namburbi rituals** — apotropaic rites that could avert the evil a sign announced;
  load-bearing for the creed reading in §6, because they show the sign was a *warning*, not a
  verdict [@rochberg2004; @kochwestenholz1995].
- **The scholars (ṭupšar Enūma Anu Enlil, "scribes of Enūma Anu Enlil")** — the professional
  readers attached to the Neo-Assyrian court; known by name from their letters and reports
  [@rochberg2004; @hungerpingree1999]. Named individuals can seed `figures/` in Phase 3.
- **The Babylonian horoscope corpus** — the c. 410 BCE-and-later personal texts
  [@rochberg2004].

## 4. What this view holds (in its own terms)

The sky is a writing surface of the gods: "the heavenly writing" (a phrase Rochberg takes for
her title from the Mesopotamian idiom) [@rochberg2004]. An omen is a message, structured as
protasis and apodosis — "if an eclipse occurs and the wind blows from the south, then…" — and
the message concerns the public order: king, harvest, war, flood [@kochwestenholz1995]. Two
features matter for everything that comes later:

1. **The sign is not the cause.** The gods write in the sky as they write in entrails or in
   dreams; the eclipse does not *make* the king's danger, it *announces* it
   [@rochberg2004]. (This is the scholarly consensus reading; stated here as the tradition's
   own logic.)
2. **The announced future is negotiable.** A bad omen triggers ritual response — the namburbi,
   or in the extreme case the substitute-king ritual — that can turn the evil aside
   [@rochberg2004; @kochwestenholz1995].

## 5. The record vs. the claims

- **"Astrology begins in Babylon"** — true only with care. What begins here is *celestial
  divination* and, very late, the horoscope; the zodiac of twelve equal signs is itself a
  Babylonian invention of c. the 5th century BCE, but the full horoscopic apparatus (houses,
  aspects, lots) is Hellenistic [@rochberg2004; @campion2008]. The dossier for the
  Hellenistic synthesis carries that story.
- **"The 70-tablet series"** — a conventional round figure; the series' exact extent varies by
  recension and modern edition [@hungerpingree1999] (confidence: medium).
- **Continuity into Greek astrology** — that Babylonian material (planetary omens, the zodiac,
  computational methods) reached the Hellenistic world is well established
  [@hungerpingree1999; @campion2008]; the precise channels are less so.

## 6. The autonomy note (the creed reads this era)

This oldest layer is, surprisingly, on the creed's side of the line: the omen announces a
*tendency* and the culture's whole ritual technology exists to keep the outcome open
[@rochberg2004]. A sign you can answer is a gate, not a fate. What it lacks is the mirror —
the reading is for the state, not the self; the individual's chart has not been invented.
Stated as history, not endorsement.
