# Jyotiṣa — The Indian Tradition

## 1. At a glance

Jyotiṣa — "the science of light" — is the Indian tradition of astronomy-and-astrology,
counted among the Vedāṅgas (the auxiliary limbs of the Veda), and it is a living, continuous
practice to this day [@pingree1997; @campion2008]. It keeps a sidereal zodiac where the West
went tropical, and it has its own apparatus: the twenty-seven nakṣatras (lunar mansions), the
daśā period systems, the nodes Rāhu and Ketu, and the deep integration of chart-reading with
ritual timing (muhūrta) and life-passage. The historiographical crux of this view is the
transmission question — how much of horoscopic Jyotiṣa came from the Hellenistic world, and
when — and that question is genuinely open in ways the grammar flags at `confidence: low`.

## 2. Chronology & geography

- A pre-horoscopic, calendrical Jyotiṣa (the *Vedāṅga Jyotiṣa* corpus, nakṣatra-based) is old
  within the Indian tradition; its dating is itself debated and reconstructed
  [@pingree1997] (confidence: low on absolute dates).
- The standard reconstruction of the horoscopic layer runs through the *Yavanajātaka*
  ("Sayings/Nativity-lore of the Greeks"): Pingree's 1978 edition argued for a Greek prose
  original translated at Alexandria-connected Ujjain c. 149/150 CE, versified by Sphujidhvaja
  c. 269/270 CE [@pingree1978].
- **That reconstruction has been substantially revised.** Bill Mak's re-examination of the
  manuscript evidence (2013) shows Pingree's key dates rest on his own emendations; the text's
  date, nature, and the tidiness of the transmission story are all looser than the textbook
  account [@mak2013]. This is the single most important correction this dossier carries.
- However dated, an Indian horoscopic literature is established by the mid-first millennium CE
  (Varāhamihira's compendia are conventionally placed 6th century CE) and develops richly and
  independently thereafter [@pingree1997; @campion2008] (confidence: medium).

## 3. Key texts & figures

- ***Vedāṅga Jyotiṣa*** — the calendrical root corpus [@pingree1997].
- ***Yavanajātaka*** — the transmission crux; Pingree's edition [@pingree1978] and Mak's
  revision [@mak2013] must be cited together from now on.
- **Varāhamihira, *Bṛhat Saṃhitā* / *Bṛhajjātaka*** — the great 6th-century compendia
  (confidence: medium on dates; cite a specialist edition before load-bearing use).
- Figures for Phase 3: Sphujidhvaja (if he existed as named), Varāhamihira — dead, eponymous,
  safe under the naming rule.

## 4. What this view holds (in its own terms)

In its own terms Jyotiṣa is not an import and not an "astrology" in the Western
compartmentalized sense: it is a limb of sacred knowledge — the eye of the Veda — through
which right action is timed and a life's karmic weather is read [@pingree1997; @campion2008].
The nakṣatra system, the daśā periods, and muhūrta election weave the sky into ritual and
daily life. Karma supplies the metaphysics: the chart shows the fruit of prior action coming
due, which — crucially for §6 — is in many strands *remediable* through rite, gift, and
conduct. (This presentation follows the standard secondary literature; a practitioner-reviewed
pass is owed — see §7.)

## 5. The record vs. the claims

- **The transmission question (the crux).** That *some* Hellenistic material entered Indian
  horoscopy in the early centuries CE is widely accepted (the very name *yavana*, "Greek,"
  is in the sources) [@pingree1997; @campion2008]. But Pingree's precise dates and his
  strong reading — a wholesale transplant, later Indianized — rest partly on emended readings
  that Mak has shown the manuscripts don't support [@mak2013]. Scholars now weight
  indigenous development more heavily, and the *Yavanajātaka*'s own date is uncertain.
  **Verdict for the grammar: keep `confidence: low`, cite both Pingree and Mak, and never
  state the 149/150 CE date as fact.**
- **"Sidereal vs. tropical"** — the zodiac divergence (ayanāṃśa) is real and defining, but
  its history is technical; avoid casual claims about "which is original."
- **"Unbroken Vedic astrology"** — the marketing claim of unchanged antiquity is not
  supported; the tradition is ancient *and* has visibly absorbed and transformed material
  [@pingree1997; @mak2013]. State both halves.

## 6. The autonomy note (the creed reads this era)

The remedial strand — that a karmic indication can be answered by rite and conduct — is this
tradition's own version of the open gate: the chart as weather report, not sentence
(presented from the secondary literature; see §7). The creed's caution cuts the other way
too: where practice hardens into binding prescription (marriage vetoes, doṣa fears), the
record should say so honestly. Stated as history, not endorsement, and owed a
practitioner-informed review.
