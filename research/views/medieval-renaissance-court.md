# Medieval & Renaissance Court Astrology

## 1. At a glance

From the 12th-century translations onward, astrology is embedded in Latin Europe's
universities, medicine, and courts: taught with astronomy in the quadrivium, used by
physicians to time treatment (iatromathematics), and kept by princes for elections and
political counsel [@tester1987; @campion2009; @grafton1999]. For centuries it is respectable
learned knowledge — while theologians police the line between the stars *inclining* the body
and *compelling* the soul, and where judicial astrology shades into forbidden determinism
[@tester1987; @thorndike1923]. The era also produces astrology's sharpest internal critic:
Pico della Mirandola's *Disputationes* (posthumous, 1496) [@pico1496].

## 2. Chronology & geography

- 12th century: the translation wave brings the Arabic corpus (Abū Maʿshar above all) into
  Latin; astrology enters the universities with the new Aristotle [@campion2009; @tester1987].
- 13th–15th centuries: astrological medicine becomes standard university fare; court
  astrologers serve across Italy, France, the Empire, England [@tester1987; @campion2009;
  @thorndike1923].
- Renaissance: the art peaks in cultural prestige — and in contestation. Cardano's career
  (1501–1576) shows the astrologer as celebrity physician-mathematician [@grafton1999].
- 1494/1496: Pico writes, and posthumously publishes, the *Disputationes adversus astrologiam
  divinatricem* [@pico1496; @campion2009].

## 4. What this view holds (in its own terms)

The learned defense runs on the Aristotelian inheritance: celestial motion governs generation
and corruption below, so the physician reads critical days, the farmer the weather, the prince
the times — a science of the *body* and of *tendencies*, with the rational soul formally exempt
[@tester1987; @campion2009]. The scholastic compromise is precisely a gate-not-fate doctrine
in period dress: the stars dispose, they do not necessitate, because grace and reason can
resist (the church's line against judicial determinism) [@tester1987; @thorndike1923].
Election and interrogation flourish anyway — the working astrologer's world Grafton
reconstructs around Cardano [@grafton1999].

## 5. The record vs. the claims

- **Pico's motive and impact** — the *Disputationes* is a genuine philosophical demolition
  (astrologers explain by the stars what proximate causes explain better), but the old story
  that it single-handedly killed learned astrology is overstated: the art thrives for another
  century-plus after 1496 [@campion2009; @grafton1999]. State his argument; do not inflate
  his effect.
- **"The Church banned astrology"** — too flat. Condemnations targeted judicial determinism;
  natural astrology (medicine, weather) remained licit and taught [@tester1987;
  @thorndike1923]. The nuance *is* the history.
- **Cardano's fate-reading of Christ's nativity** (the notorious scandal) — attested in the
  scholarship [@grafton1999] but load-bearing details should be quoted from Grafton directly,
  not from memory (confidence: medium).

## 6. The autonomy note (the creed reads this era)

The scholastic formula — the stars incline the complexion, never compel the will — is the
direct medieval ancestor of this project's creed, developed to keep human freedom intact
inside a cosmos of influence [@tester1987]. The court practice often ignored the formula;
the record keeps both the doctrine and the breach. Stated as history, not endorsement.
