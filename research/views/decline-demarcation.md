# The Great Decline & the Demarcation

## 1. At a glance

Between the sixteenth and eighteenth centuries astrology loses its standing in Europe's
learned culture: out of the universities, out of elite medicine, out of respectable natural
philosophy — surviving vigorously below, in almanacs and popular practice [@curry1989;
@thomas1971; @campion2009]. The usual story says the Scientific Revolution refuted it; the
historians' story is slower and more social: the physics that carried celestial influence
dissolved, elite patronage drifted away, and "science versus astrology" was drawn as a
boundary in the very act of defining the new natural philosophy [@curry1989; @thomas1971].
The demarcation is itself a historical artifact — which is why it earns a *view* in this
library rather than a verdict over it.

## 2. Chronology & geography

- 1543: Copernicus, *De revolutionibus*; 1687: Newton's *Principia* — the conventional
  bookends of the cosmological shift [@campion2009]. Neither text is, on its own, an
  anti-astrology event; the erosion is downstream and gradual.
- Mid-17th century England is the paradox that proves the social reading: astrology's
  greatest English flowering — William Lilly's *Christian Astrology* (1647), the almanac
  boom, civil-war prophecy — happens *inside* the century of its elite collapse [@lilly1647;
  @curry1989; @thomas1971].
- By the early 18th century, judicial astrology has no place in the Royal Society's world;
  the practice continues in the popular almanac trade [@curry1989; @campion2009].

## 3. Key texts & figures

- **William Lilly, *Christian Astrology* (1647)** — the great English manual, PD, on
  archive.org [@lilly1647]; both a monument of the working tradition and, in hindsight, its
  elite swan song.
- **The almanacs** — the mass print genre that carries astrology through and past the
  decline; Curry's books are the studies to cite [@curry1989; @curry1992].
- **Keith Thomas, *Religion and the Decline of Magic* (1971)** — the classic account of how
  a whole world of practices, astrology included, lost its plausibility structure
  [@thomas1971].
- Figures for Phase 3: Lilly (d. 1681) — dead, eponymous, and his book PD.

## 4. What this view holds (in its own terms)

As a *view*, the demarcation holds that astrology and the new science are different kinds of
knowledge and must be separated — that the question "does the sky cause events below?" now
belongs to a physics that answers no. Its own terms are boundary-drawing terms: demonstration
against conjecture, mechanism against correspondence [@campion2009; @curry1989]. The view's
historiographical honesty requires noticing that the boundary was drawn by people with
stakes, over time, and unevenly — Curry shows English astrology dying socially (patronage,
respectability) rather than being executed by a single experiment [@curry1989; @thomas1971].

## 5. The record vs. the claims

- **"Copernicus/Newton refuted astrology"** — no single refutation event exists in the
  record; the claim compresses a century-plus of social and intellectual drift
  [@curry1989; @campion2009]. The grammar's phrasing ("not a single refutation on a single
  day") is the accurate one; keep it.
- **"Astrology died"** — false below the elite line: the almanac trade and popular practice
  continue robustly into the 18th–19th centuries [@curry1989; @curry1992].
- **Astronomers' own entanglement** — early modern astronomers (Kepler most famously) worked
  both sides of the later boundary; a load-bearing claim about Kepler's exact stance needs
  its own sourced pass — widely discussed, easily overstated (confidence: medium; no
  citation offered here rather than a loose one).

## 6. The autonomy note (the creed reads this era)

The demarcation is the critique's second act (after Pico), and the creed takes from it
something it affirms: the sky-as-cause claim did not survive examination, and this library
never revives it. What the creed declines is the demarcation's occasional overreach — reading
five millennia of meaning-making as mere error. The history of reading the sky remains a
subject after the physics is gone. Stated as history, not endorsement (of either side's
maximal claim).
