# Chapter 3 — The archive that became astronomy

## One-paragraph answer

Mesopotamian celestial divination was never separated from what we'd call astronomy —
the omen tradition (canonized as *Enūma Anu Enlil*, ~2nd millennium BCE onward) gave
scribes a *reason* to watch the sky nightly for centuries, and that motivated watching
produced the Astronomical Diaries: a continuous, dated observational record running from
roughly 652 BCE to 61 BCE, the longest research program from the ancient world. That
archive is the empirical bedrock later Greek astronomy drew on — most concretely,
Ptolemy's *Almagest* cites Babylonian eclipse records reaching back to 747 BCE (the era
of Nabonassar), and the scholarly consensus (Toomer, Aaboe) holds that Hipparchus is the
conduit through which Babylonian eclipse data and period relations (the "System B"
parameters) entered Greek mathematical astronomy. The same archive and the same scribal
class *also* produced the goal-year texts, System A/B ephemerides — and, from as early
as 410 BCE, the first individual horoscopes. One data-generating practice, motivated by
meaning, forked into both mathematical astronomy and horoscopic astrology. There is no
point at which "science" peels cleanly away from "meaning" — the meaning-grammar is what
built the dataset the data-grammar depends on.

## Claims, confidence, and sources

**1. Enūma Anu Enlil — the omen series and its date range.**
✔︎ *Enūma Anu Enlil* ("When the gods Anu and Enlil...") is the great Mesopotamian
celestial-omen series: roughly 70 tablets, ~7,000 omens, covering lunar, solar,
meteorological, planetary, and stellar phenomena. ○ Its scattered antecedents go back to
the Old Babylonian period (~1950–1595 BCE); ◆ scholars differ on exactly when a
"canonical" 70-tablet recension was fixed — sources place standardization anywhere from
the Kassite period (~1595–1157 BCE) through later redaction, with an attested catalogue
from 7th-c. BCE Assur and another from 3rd-c. BCE Uruk, meaning the series was still being
copied and catalogued into the Hellenistic era. Point for the chapter: this is a
*divinatory* text — omens are conditional statements ("if the moon appears at its
expected time, the land will be secure"), not physical descriptions. It is the motivating
grammar, not the data record itself. [Wikipedia: Enuma Anu Enlil; Rochberg, *The
Catalogues of Enūma Anu Enlil*]

**2. The Astronomical Diaries — span, content, location, editions.**
✔︎ The Diaries run from the earliest surviving tablet (652 BCE) to the last (61 BCE) —
close to 600 years, with the bulk surviving from the Hellenistic period (331–141 BCE).
✔︎ Each entry recorded, for a given night/month: positions and phenomena of the Moon and
planets relative to fixed "Normal Stars," eclipses, solstices/equinoxes, the "Lunar Six"
timing intervals — and, at each month's end, non-astronomical data: the Euphrates river
level, market prices of six commodities (barley, dates, mustard/cuscuta, cress, sesame,
wool), weather (wind, fog, haze, lightning), and notable political/military events. ✔︎
Most of the several hundred surviving tablets are held in the British Museum (excavated
at Babylon in the late 19th century; a further trove was identified by Abraham Sachs
during 1950s work there). ✔︎ The standard scholarly edition is Sachs & Hunger,
*Astronomical Diaries and Related Texts from Babylonia* (Österreichische Akademie der
Wissenschaften), published across multiple volumes (Sachs began the project; Hermann
Hunger completed it after Sachs's death), Vol. I covering 652–262 BCE. [Wikipedia:
Babylonian astronomical diaries; Livius.org, "Astronomical Diaries"; BMCR review of
Steele, *Keeping Watch in Babylon*]

**3. Goal-year texts and System A/B mathematical astronomy.**
✔︎ Out of the raw observational stream grew derivative genres: Goal-Year Texts (excerpts
of past observations arranged by planetary period so a scribe could predict a planet's
behavior in a future "goal year" when the same period recurs — earliest attested ~256
BCE) and Almanacs/Normal Star Almanacs (predictions, earliest ~282 BCE) that applied
those period relations. ✔︎ The most sophisticated layer is the ACT ("Astronomical
Cuneiform Texts") corpus: ephemerides computing positions of Sun, Moon, and planets by
two distinct arithmetic schemes conventionally labeled System A and System B, plus
procedure texts describing the methods. ◆ How exactly the non-mathematical observational
texts (Diaries, Goal-Year Texts) fed the purely computational System A/B schemes is
called, in the secondary literature itself, "one of the major unsolved problems" in
Babylonian astronomy — the arithmetic models are not simple curve-fits to the Diaries,
but the period parameters they encode were clearly refined against centuries of exactly
this kind of observation. [Wikipedia: Babylonian astronomy; Steele/Hunger-Pingree
scholarship on goal-year astronomy]

**4. The transmission claim — Hipparchus, Babylonian eclipses, Ptolemy. (Load-bearing; state confidence honestly.)**
✔︎ Ptolemy's *Almagest* explicitly cites Babylonian eclipse observations, including ones
reaching back to the era of Nabonassar (Ptolemy's own chronological epoch, beginning 26
February 747 BCE) — some of these eclipse records match tablets that independently
survive (e.g., the 16 July 523 BCE eclipse recorded on tablet BM 33066). Ptolemy is using
data more than 800 years old by his own time to calibrate lunar theory. ◆ The
mechanism connecting Babylon to Ptolemy is Hipparchus (2nd c. BCE), and here the
confidence has to be more careful: G. J. Toomer ("Hipparchus's Empirical Basis for His
Lunar Mean Motions," *Centaurus* 24, 1980) argued that Hipparchus compiled a list of
Babylonian eclipse and other observations and used them directly — comparing eclipses
from his own era against Babylonian eclipses roughly 345 years earlier to derive precise
lunar period relations — and that Ptolemy's Babylonian material in the Almagest came via
Hipparchus's compilation rather than Ptolemy consulting Babylonian sources independently.
◆ This is the scholarly majority view but not textually airtight: the only surviving
Hipparchus text does not itself prove he used Babylonian material, and (per the
secondary literature) the one cuneiform tablet that could bear on direction-of-transfer
is dated *after* Hipparchus's lifetime — so the tablet evidence alone doesn't settle
which way information flowed on that specific point. ✔︎ Separately, it's established that
the period relations Ptolemy attributes to Hipparchus for the planets are the same
period relations already present in the Babylonian "System B" ephemerides — a
independent, converging line of evidence for transmission, distinct from the eclipse-list
argument. ✔︎ Asger Aaboe's broader claim — "all subsequent varieties of scientific
astronomy, in the Hellenistic world, in India, in Islam, and in the West... depend upon
Babylonian astronomy in decisive and fundamental ways" — is a strong, often-quoted
historian's synthesis; treat it as a scholarly thesis, not an uncontested fact, but one
with real evidentiary weight behind it (Toomer's eclipse-list argument, the System
B/Hipparchus period-relation match, and Ptolemy's own explicit citations all point the
same direction). [Toomer 1980, *Centaurus*; Wikipedia: Hipparchus, Nabonassar,
Babylonian astronomy; Steele, "Visual Aspects of the Transmission of Babylonian
Astronomy... into Greek Astronomy," *Annals of Science* 2010]

**5. The honest complication — the same archive fed horoscopy too.**
✔︎ The earliest known individual (as opposed to state/omen) horoscope is dated to 29
April 410 BCE — a cuneiform tablet recording planetary positions at a birth (Moon below
the Scorpio pincers, Jupiter in Pisces, Venus in Taurus, Saturn in Cancer, Mars in
Gemini, Mercury not visible) for the purpose of forecasting that individual's health,
marriage, and fate. ✔︎ Over 30 such horoscopes survive from the 5th–1st centuries BCE
(Rochberg's *Babylonian Horoscopes*, the standard edition/study). ✔︎ This is a genuine
fork *from the same observational and computational infrastructure* — the same scribal
schools, the same ephemeris techniques (System A/B), the same Normal Star reference
frame used in the Diaries — redirected from state-level omen judgment toward personal
nativities. ✔︎ Following Alexander's conquest (331 BCE), this horoscopic strand is what
spread into the Hellenistic world and fused with Greek mathematics/philosophy to become
the horoscopic astrology the West inherited — running in parallel with, not
subordinate to, the mathematical-astronomy strand that fed Hipparchus and Ptolemy.
[Rochberg, *Babylonian Horoscopes* (Penn Press); Grokipedia/secondary summaries of
Rochberg's dating; Rochberg, *In the Path of the Moon* and *Before Nature* — the
latter's central thesis, per reviews, is that Babylonian scribal knowledge worked as an
integrated system of signs and judgments with no native concept of "nature" separating
divination from what we'd call empirical science]

## What the chapter should say

- The nightly-watching *habit* — centuries of it, uninterrupted, dated, archived — did
  not arise despite the omen tradition; it arose *because of* it. *Enūma Anu Enlil* gave
  scribes a standing reason (reading the god-given signs of state) to keep watching long
  after any one omen's "prediction" had cashed out or not. Motivation and data are not
  opposed; the motivation produced the data.
- The Astronomical Diaries are safely, verifiably the longest continuous observational
  record from the ancient world, and Ptolemy's own text visibly depends on centuries-old
  Babylonian eclipse reports. That much is solid ground.
- The Hipparchus mechanism should be stated as the field's best-supported reconstruction,
  not a settled fact — "the majority scholarly view, built on converging lines of
  evidence, with acknowledged gaps in the tablet record" is the honest register. This
  chapter's whole point is modeling that kind of calibrated confidence, so don't
  overclaim the one link (Hipparchus-as-conduit) that's still argued.
- Land the anti-flattening point explicitly: the same tablets, the same schools, the same
  math produced *both* Ptolemy's planetary tables *and* the first personal horoscope five
  centuries earlier. If the meaning-grammar were mere superstition standing in the way of
  "real" astronomy, it should have produced only noise. Instead it's the trunk both
  branches — astronomy and astrology — grow from. That is the chapter's thesis, not a
  caveat on it.

## What the chapter should not say

- Should not claim Hipparchus's use of Babylonian data is proven beyond dispute by a
  named surviving tablet — the direction-of-transmission tablet evidence is explicitly
  incomplete per the secondary literature.
- Should not claim a single fixed "canonical edition date" for *Enūma Anu Enlil* — the
  compilation history is genuinely contested/layered across a millennium or more.
- Should not narrate this as "astrology accidentally invented astronomy" (still a
  ranking) or as "it was all really astronomy wearing a religious costume" (erases the
  omen-series' own terms). Both directions flatten. The honest claim is co-production:
  one long observational practice, two legitimate, non-competing descendants.
- Should not omit that Babylonian omen texts remained in active use and recopying
  alongside — not superseded by — the mathematical astronomy strand; this wasn't
  meaning giving way to data but meaning and data running concurrently.
