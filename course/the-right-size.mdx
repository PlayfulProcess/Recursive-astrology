# The Right Size

*The human imperative to negotiate our scale — and how the sky might be an adaptive path*

## The problem is not that we are small

The trouble with being human is that we are almost exactly the wrong size to know how big we are. Small enough that a virus can end us, large enough that we have learned to end almost everything else. We built fire, then engines, then bombs, then machines that can write — and at every step the power arrived faster than the wisdom to hold it. A great deal of human suffering, I have come to think, is just this: a species that keeps mistaking its size. When we forget how small we are, we call it hubris, and it burns things down. When we forget how remarkable we are, we call it despair, and it lets things rot. Between those two errors there is a narrow, difficult place — the right size — and almost everything a culture makes, from its laws to its festivals to its art, is a device for helping people find it and stay there.

I want to say plainly, at the start, what this course does not claim. It does not claim the planets decide anything. Whether the position of Mars on the night you were born reached down and set the shape of your life — no, not as far as anyone has ever been able to show, and I will keep faith with that finding throughout. But I think that question, asked first, quietly ruins everything after it. It treats the sky the way an auditor treats a ledger: does it pay out or doesn't it. That was never what looking up was for. The older question, the warmer one, is not *can the sky know my future.* It is: *what happens to us when we agree to relate to the sky as though it were alive?*

## What we cannot know, and do not need to

Here is the honest ground. We do not know whether a planet has anything like an inner life. We do not know whether the word *spirit* names something real or something we do beautifully to ourselves. These are not questions I can settle for you, and — this is the part that took me years to trust — we do not need them settled in order to proceed. The value of a practice almost never waits on the metaphysics behind it. You do not have to prove the world is alive before you can live as though it is. You just relate, and the relating does its work, and the work is real whatever the truth turns out to be.

What we *can* do is agree to build a shared world on top of the one we can measure — a secondary world, drawn over the real one like a map over territory. Every people that ever lived has done this. We take the raw, indifferent sky and we populate it: this bright point is a warrior, that slow one a teacher, this pale disc a mother who governs the tides and the months. None of it is discovered in the rock of the planets. All of it is agreed. And agreement, it turns out, is not a lesser thing than truth. It is how we manage to live together at all.

That is why I think of astrology less as a claim about the heavens and more as a kind of social contract — a set of shared meanings we consent to, so that strangers can coordinate their inner lives the way a currency lets them coordinate their outer ones. And like any social contract, we get to choose what kind it will be. We can write it as cold law — rigid, literal, a machine that files each person into a box and calls the box destiny. Or we can write it as poetry: a language loose enough to leave the cracks in, open enough that no one mistakes the map for the world. The first kind produces the astrology that deserves its critics. The second produces something older and better — a way of holding our own lives at arm's length long enough to see them, and of remembering, every time we look up, that we are not the largest thing in the room.

## The sun does not care, and that is the first lesson

Begin with the one star we cannot argue about. The sun is a ball of fusing hydrogen so large that a million Earths would fit inside it, and it is, in the most literal and unromantic sense, the reason you are alive. Nearly every calorie in your body was sunlight first, caught by a leaf and handed down. Your sleep runs on a clock set by its rising; take that clock away and the body sickens within days. The sunflower keeps its own copy of that clock inside its stem, so that it turns to face east before the light arrives, anticipating a sunrise it cannot yet see (Atamian et al., *Science*, 2016). We did not decide any of this. It is simply true, and it is enormous, and it does not know we are here.

That indifference is the first lesson in scale. The sun is not a father watching over you; it is a furnace that would incinerate you without noticing, and it is also, at exactly the same time, the thing that grows your food and wakes your body and paints every color you have ever seen. To stand in the morning light and feel *both* of those at once — how small this makes me, how alive this makes me — is not superstition. It is accuracy. It is the correct emotional response to a true fact about your size. The old word for that response is awe, and awe, I want to argue, is not a decoration on top of understanding. It is a form of understanding. It is what right-sized knowledge feels like from the inside.

## When we relate to Mars as war, we make war a little more real

Consider the red planet. Mars is a cold desert with a thin, poisonous air; it is not angry, and it has never wanted anything. But for as long as we have had records, humans across unrelated cultures looked at that rust-colored point and agreed to call it the warrior — Ares, Mars, Mangala, Nergal. And here is the strange, important thing: once enough of us relate to Mars as war, war becomes a little more available to us. The word organizes a feeling; the feeling organizes a season of raids, a name for a child, a temper we grant ourselves permission to have. The planet did not make it so. Our shared agreement did. We drew the meaning, and then we lived inside our own drawing.

This is not a bug in astrology; it is the whole engine, and it is worth being clear-eyed about because it cuts both ways. The same mechanism that can make Mars into an excuse for cruelty can make Venus into permission for tenderness, or Saturn into the patience to sit with a hard limit instead of raging at it. A symbol we hold in common becomes a handle on our own behavior. Christopher Wallis, writing from within the non-dual Shaiva Tantra tradition, describes the personal deities of that path not as literal beings sitting above the world but as the forms a single boundless consciousness *takes on* so that a human being can relate to it — a face put on the faceless, precisely so we have something to love and orient by. "Consciousness," he says, "comes in all these flavors we call deities, gods, and goddesses." A planet held as a god is the same move: not a claim that Mars is alive, but a decision to give the vast and the indifferent a face we can answer to.

Harari makes the secular version of this point about money and nations and law — shared fictions that are not "true" in the way a rock is true, and that nevertheless run the world, because enough of us agree to act as if they were. Astrology is a fiction of exactly that kind. Its one quiet advantage is that its anchor is *visible.* You and a stranger on the far side of the earth cannot agree on which government is legitimate or which god is real, but you can both walk outside tonight and see the same moon, and check each other against it. Of all the imagined orders we have coordinated our lives around, the sky is the only one everyone can point to.

## Even the strictest thinkers left the metaphysics alone

If this feels like sleight of hand — *relating to planets as if they were beings you don't actually believe in* — it may help to know that some of the most rigorous minds in history stood exactly here and were untroubled. The Mīmāṃsā philosophers of classical India were ritualists so austere they make a modern skeptic look sentimental. On their analysis, the mythic stories attached to a rite carried no independent truth at all; the gods invoked had, in the strict view, no existence apart from the words used to invoke them. And yet they held the ritual to be worth doing with total seriousness — because the *practice* was where the value lived, not in any metaphysical claim standing behind it. The temple did not need to be provably sacred. What happened in the people gathered inside it was the whole of the matter.

The economist Milton Friedman, from a wholly different world, gave the cleaner modern statement of the same permission. Leaves on a tree, he noted, arrange themselves *as if* each one were deliberately calculating how to catch the most sunlight — and a model that treats them that way will predict their positions beautifully, even though everyone knows a leaf deliberates nothing. Whether the "as if" is literally true is not the point; whether it is *useful* is. So too here. We can relate to the sky as if it were animate, and gain what that relating gives, without ever having to pretend we have settled a question no one has settled. You do not need a metaphysical permission slip to love something. You never did.

## The visible calendar we can all keep

Strip away every claim and one plain fact remains: the sky is the best clock our species has ever shared. Coral colonies along a thousand miles of reef release their eggs on the same few nights, cued by the phase of the moon (the lunar timing is one of the most solidly documented rhythms in biology). The tides breathe on the pull of the moon and sun; the seasons turn on the tilt of a visible sky. Long before writing, we planted and harvested and gathered by these signals, and — crucially — we built *festivals* on them: solstices, new moons, the heliacal rising of a star that told the Nile it was time to flood. A festival is a coordination technology. It is how a whole community comes to *know that everyone else knows* it is time to gather, so that everyone actually does (this is the game theorist Michael Chwe's precise account of public ritual: shared events manufacture common knowledge, and common knowledge is what lets large groups act as one).

And when we do gather, in time with one another, something measurable happens to us. Moving in synchrony — a drum, a dance, a shared calendar — reliably increases how much people trust and cooperate with each other afterward (Wiltermuth & Heath, *Psychological Science*, 2009); Durkheim called the charge that rises off a gathered, synchronized crowd *collective effervescence*, and thought it was the origin of the sense of the sacred itself. Here, then, is a benefit that requires no belief in the stars whatsoever. Timing our lives to the visible heavens binds us to each other. The sky is the one calendar no emperor issued and no church owns, the one every human being can read from their own doorstep — and agreeing to keep it together is, all by itself, a real and repeatable good.

I will not oversell it. The single astrological claim closest to genuine causation — that the moon's phase measurably swings human behavior, births, moods, madness — is the one that, tested carefully across dozens of studies, mostly is not there (Rotton & Kelly's meta-analysis found the moon explains, at most, a fraction of one percent of the variance in anything). The gravitational tug of Mars on a newborn is real but is dwarfed a thousandfold by the doctor standing beside the crib. So let me be exact about the honest claim. The sky does not push our lives around. It is real, it is shared, it is the oldest thing we all still look at — and relating to it well can change us, the way relating to anything well can change us, from the inside out.

## Is the aliveness only here?

There is one door I want to leave open, carefully, because it presses on the whole question of scale. If there is anything to the intuition that mind or spirit is real — and a serious minority of contemporary philosophers, under the name panpsychism, argue that consciousness may be a basic feature of the world rather than a late trick of brains — then I have never understood why we would assume it stops at the edge of Earth's atmosphere. If spirit does not depend on our particular biology, it has no obvious reason to be provincial. The same person will often say, without noticing the strain, that they believe in souls but not in intelligent life elsewhere in a universe of a hundred billion galaxies. I do not know the answer. I only notice the inconsistency, and I notice that the culture is quietly reopening the question: the taboo around unidentified aerial phenomena has broken in the last few years, with government hearings and official reports treating "are we alone" as a question one is now permitted to ask in daylight.

I raise this not as a claim but as a discipline of humility — the very thing this course is about. To hold the sky as possibly, unprovably animate is to keep ourselves the right size: not the sole authors of meaning in a dead cosmos, and not the passive subjects of a fate written in the stars, but one small, wondering species among who-knows-what, drawing our maps and holding them lightly. The point is not to believe the planets are alive. The point is to refuse the arrogance of being certain they are not.

## The sky as an adaptive path

So let me gather the whole thing into one sentence, and it is not a sentence about the stars. It is a sentence about us. We are a species that keeps getting more powerful than it is wise, and our oldest, cheapest, most democratic instrument for staying the right size is the simple act of looking up. The sky humbles us and includes us at once. It asks nothing, owns nothing, predicts nothing — and offers itself, freely, as a shared secondary world we can choose to coordinate by: a calendar for our festivals, a cast of characters for our inner weather, a nightly reminder that we did not make the light we live by.

We get to choose how we hold it. We can turn it into cold law and cruel certainty, one more machine for filing human beings into boxes. Or we can hold it as the Brazilian imagination has so often taught — with warmth, with play, with room for the cracks, aspiring not to master the whole but to take our right and proper place inside it. The sacred, in the end, is not a property of the planets. It is the quality of awe with which we relate to a whole so much larger than ourselves — merely human, and at the very same time wonderfully, improbably, magically human. The light was never in the stars, waiting. It was always in the looking up, together, and in what the looking makes of us.

*A note on honesty, in the spirit of the whole Recursive project: nothing in this course asks you to believe the planets influence your life, and where the evidence is genuinely mixed or absent I have tried to say so plainly rather than let a lovely sentence stand in for a fact. The philosophy — Wallis on deity as a form consciousness takes, the Mīmāṃsā view of ritual, Friedman's "as if," Harari on shared fictions, Chwe on common knowledge, panpsychism as a live minority position — is offered as a way of holding the practice, not as proof of anything above the atmosphere. The metaphysics we cannot know; the relating we can.*
