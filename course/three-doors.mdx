# Three Doors

*Reading the sky through the Trika — a synthesis, offered by a non-scholar*

## A confession before the door

Let me say the honest thing first, because the whole reading depends on it. I am not a scholar of the Trika, and no tradition anywhere teaches what I am about to do with it. The Trika is a real and precise structure of non-dual Kashmir Shaivism — three goddesses, Parā, Parāparā, and Aparā, mapped by Abhinavagupta a thousand years ago onto the whole architecture of consciousness. What follows is not that. It is me, a Brazilian woman who reads the sky, borrowing three doors from a grammar I love and using them to walk into a chart. If you want the tradition, go to it — [Christopher Wallis's *Tantra Illuminated*](https://www.tantrikstudies.org/) is where I first met these goddesses, and they are far larger than my use of them. Take what follows as a lens I ground for myself, and offer to you, holding it as lightly as I hope you will.

## The first door: the light that looks (Parā)

Parā is the supreme goddess — awareness itself, the light in which everything appears, before it has split into someone seeing and something seen. Through her door you do not read a chart for what it will do to you. You read it for the one who is reading it. Every planet on the wheel is appearing to a consciousness that is not on the wheel — you, looking. The ascendant, that thin line where the sky meets the self, is her natural home: not your fate, but the angle from which the whole of it is seen. To begin in Parā is to ask, before a single interpretation: *who is looking?* What in me would still be here if every placement were different? Start there, and nothing the sky says can shrink you.

## The third door: the world that is looked at (Aparā)

Aparā is the immanent goddess — the same supreme light, now fully committed to being something specific: this body, this hour, this difficulty you did not choose. She is not lesser. She is consciousness with its sleeves rolled up. Through her door you read the concrete givens — the houses and their affairs, the transits actually arriving, the weather of an actual life. This is the door astrology usually opens first, and it is also where astrology goes wrong: when Aparā is taken *alone*, the given hardens into fate and a reading becomes a sentence. So read here honestly, and read here only halfway. These are the materials — real, weighty, not to be argued with. They are not a verdict. They are what you have to work with.

## The middle door, which is the whole point (Parāparā)

And now the door almost no one talks about, and the reason I made this reading at all. Parāparā is the goddess of the middle — supreme-and-not-supreme at once, neither the light nor the world but the living *relation between them*, the place where the one who looks and the thing looked at actually touch. In a chart, she is exactly what the aspects are: planets in dialogue, angles of tension and grace, the sky in conversation with itself and with you. And here is what the middle door tells you: a reading is not the awareness alone and not the circumstance alone. It is what happens in the space where they meet and make something of each other. Read in Parāparā and astrology quietly stops being prediction and becomes what it always was underneath — a practice of relationship. Not *who am I*, and not *what is happening*, but: what is being asked of the meeting between them? What would it mean to greet this given, from this awareness, well? That answer is never on the wheel. It is made in the meeting — every time, and only there.

## Why I needed a third door

I spent a long time thinking astrology had two settings: the sceptic's, where the sky is dead and reading it is a mistake, and the believer's, where the sky is a script and reading it is obedience. Both of those live behind the third door — Aparā, the world, taken as the only door there is. The Trika gave me the other two, and the middle one broke the false choice open. The light that looks is free; the world that is looked at is real; and the relationship between them is where a human being actually lives, and loves, and decides. That is the sky I want to keep — not a fate written in the stars, and not a nothing, but a third thing, made in the looking, between us and the vast indifferent beautiful given. The moon was never going to tell me what to do. The moon, it turns out, was relationship all along.
